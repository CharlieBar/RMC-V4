The medical side of a personal injury case raises questions that most people have never had to think about before, and getting clear answers quickly makes an enormous difference in both your health and your legal outcome.

At Rand Medical Center in Arlington Heights, IL, we treat patients from car accidents, workplace incidents, slip-and-fall injuries, and other personal injury cases every day. ## Do I Need an Attorney Before I Can See a Doctor?

No. You do not need an attorney to begin medical treatment after an accident. In fact, waiting for legal representation before seeking care is one of the most common and costly mistakes injury patients make.

Your health is the immediate priority. Some injuries — particularly soft tissue injuries to the neck and back — do not produce their full range of symptoms immediately after an accident. Pain, stiffness, and neurological symptoms often worsen over the first 24 to 72 hours. Seeking evaluation promptly means those symptoms are documented as they develop, which is important both medically and legally.

From a legal standpoint, the sooner your injuries are documented in a medical record, the stronger the connection between the accident and your condition. Delays in treatment can be used by insurance adjusters to argue that your injuries were not serious or were caused by something else entirely.

If you do not yet have an attorney when you come to see us, we can still provide thorough documentation. Your records will be available for you or any future attorney to use. Many of our patients hire legal representation after their initial evaluation, and we coordinate seamlessly with attorneys at that point.

If you were injured in an accident in the Arlington Heights area and have not yet been seen by a physician, contact our [immediate care](/services/immediate-care) team as soon as possible.

## How Will I Pay for Treatment?

This is one of the most important practical questions, and the answer depends on the nature of your case and the coverage you have available.

**Lien-based treatment:** For qualified personal injury and [auto accident cases](/services/injury-services/motor-vehicle-accidents), Rand Medical Center can often provide treatment on a lien basis. Under this arrangement, you receive treatment now and payment is deferred until your case settles. This means financial concerns do not have to delay your care.

**Auto insurance medical payments (MedPay):** Many auto insurance policies include a MedPay provision that covers medical expenses resulting from a car accident regardless of fault. This coverage is often underutilized because patients are unaware it exists. Check your auto policy or call your insurance agent to find out if you have MedPay coverage.

**Health insurance:** Your standard health insurance can be used for personal injury treatment in many cases. If your insurer pays for treatment that is later the subject of a settlement, they may assert a subrogation claim — meaning they ask to be reimbursed from your settlement proceeds. Your attorney can advise on how this works in your specific situation.

**Workers' compensation:** If your injury occurred at work, [workers' compensation](/services/injury-services/workers-compensation) insurance covers your medical treatment at no out-of-pocket cost to you. You should not need to use your personal health insurance for a work-related injury.

## How Soon Should I Be Seen After an Accident?

As soon as possible. Ideally within 24 to 48 hours of the incident, and certainly within the first week.

There are two reasons for this, and both are significant.

**Medical reasons:** The inflammatory response following soft tissue trauma develops over time. Many patients feel relatively little pain immediately after an accident — adrenaline, shock, and the body's initial protective responses can mask symptoms. As inflammation develops over the following 48 to 72 hours, pain, stiffness, headaches, and neurological symptoms often emerge or worsen considerably. Early evaluation establishes your baseline and allows your physician to monitor this progression, which is important for accurate diagnosis and appropriate treatment.

Conditions like [neck pain](/conditions/spine/neck-pain), [back pain](/conditions/spine/back-pain), and radiating nerve symptoms from disc injuries may not fully declare themselves for several days. An experienced physician evaluating you early can identify early signs of these injuries before they become chronic problems.

**Legal and documentation reasons:** Insurance adjusters look for gaps in treatment as evidence that injuries were not serious. A documented gap between the accident and your first medical visit gives them an argument that your injuries were minor, resolved quickly, or were caused by something else. Starting your medical record promptly eliminates this vulnerability.

## What If I Didn't Go to the Emergency Room?

That's entirely okay, and it is extremely common. Many accident victims do not go to the emergency room, particularly when symptoms are moderate, develop gradually, or seem manageable in the immediate aftermath of the accident.

Emergency rooms are equipped to rule out life-threatening conditions — serious fractures, internal bleeding, head trauma. They are generally not equipped to provide the ongoing evaluation and management that most personal injury cases require. A patient discharged from an ER with a note reading "cervical strain, follow up with your doctor" still needs that follow-up — and that follow-up is exactly what we provide.

If you did not go to the ER, come to Rand Medical Center directly. We will perform a thorough evaluation, obtain any necessary imaging such as X-rays or MRI referrals, document your injuries completely, and begin your treatment plan. The fact that you did not go to the ER first does not diminish the value of your medical documentation or your claim.

If you did go to the ER, bring those records with you to your first appointment. They provide useful context for your treating physician and help establish continuity of care.

## What Kind of Documentation Do You Provide?

Thorough, organized, and legally useful documentation is a cornerstone of what Rand Medical Center provides in personal injury cases. We understand that the quality of your medical records can meaningfully affect the outcome of your case.

**Standard medical records:** Every visit generates a clinical note documenting your subjective complaints, objective findings on examination, assessment and diagnosis, and the treatment plan. These records are the foundation of your case.

**Diagnostic imaging reports:** If you undergo X-rays, MRI scans, or other imaging, the radiologist's written report becomes part of your record. We can also provide the actual images on disc.

**Treatment logs:** Detailed records of every physical therapy session, injection procedure, or other treatment — including your response to treatment over time.

**Progress notes:** Periodic documentation of how your condition has changed, plateaued, or worsened during the course of treatment.

**Narrative medical reports:** A comprehensive document authored by your treating physician that synthesizes your entire medical history with us, explains your injuries in plain language, describes the treatment you have received, and provides an opinion on causation and prognosis. This is often the most valuable document in a personal injury case.

**Disability documentation:** If your injuries prevent you from working or limit your activities, your physician can document functional limitations and work restrictions.

All of these documents can be provided to you, your attorney, or authorized insurance carriers. We follow all HIPAA requirements, and any release of records requires your signed authorization.

## What Types of Injuries Do You Treat?

Rand Medical Center [injury services](/services/injury-services) covers a broad range of conditions commonly seen in personal injury cases:

**Spinal injuries:** [Neck pain](/conditions/spine/neck-pain) and [back pain](/conditions/spine/back-pain) from whiplash and soft tissue trauma are among the most common injuries in auto accidents. We also evaluate and treat herniated discs, radiculopathy, and other spinal conditions that result from traumatic impact.

**Joint injuries:** Shoulder, knee, hip, and wrist injuries frequently occur in accidents and falls. Our orthopedic team evaluates joint injuries and determines whether conservative treatment or surgical intervention is appropriate.

**Nerve injuries:** Shooting pain, numbness, tingling, and weakness can indicate nerve involvement — either from direct trauma or from swelling compressing nerve structures. These symptoms require careful evaluation.

**Soft tissue injuries:** Muscle strains, ligament sprains, and tendon injuries are common in accidents. While these may be dismissed as "minor," they can cause significant pain and functional limitation and often require weeks of [physical therapy](/services/physical-therapy) to resolve properly.

**Chronic pain:** When injuries are not treated promptly or adequately, acute pain can transition into chronic pain conditions. Our [pain management](/services/pain-management) team has extensive experience addressing pain that has persisted beyond the expected healing timeframe.

## Practical Tips for Personal Injury Patients

These steps will protect your health and support your case:

**Document everything from day one.** Keep a daily journal noting your pain levels, symptoms, how your injuries are affecting your sleep, work, and daily activities, and any medications you are taking. This contemporaneous record is valuable evidence.

**Photograph your injuries.** If you have visible injuries — bruising, lacerations, swelling — photograph them regularly as they evolve. Photos from multiple days show the progression of your injuries.

**Attend every appointment.** Gaps in treatment are the single biggest vulnerability in personal injury cases. Consistent attendance demonstrates that your injuries are genuine and that you are taking your recovery seriously.

**Follow all instructions.** If your physician recommends activity restrictions, take them seriously. If you are prescribed home exercises, do them. Compliance with your treatment plan affects both your recovery and the credibility of your claim.

**Keep all receipts and records.** Out-of-pocket medical expenses, prescription costs, transportation to appointments, and other accident-related costs may be recoverable. Save every receipt.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I treat at Rand Medical Center if I already have an attorney?

Yes. Many patients come to us after they have already retained legal counsel. We work directly with attorneys and can coordinate with your legal team from the beginning of your care. If your attorney has specific documentation requirements or preferred formats for records, let us know early in the process.

### What is a "letter of protection" and do you offer them?

A letter of protection (LOP) is a document from your attorney guaranteeing payment for medical services from the proceeds of your case settlement. In appropriate cases, we can accept a letter of protection as a payment arrangement. This is particularly useful when a patient does not have health insurance and the case has not yet resolved. Contact our office to discuss whether an LOP arrangement is available for your situation.

### Will my treatment records be used against me?

Your records document your injuries, treatment, and progress — which is generally in your favor. Thorough records demonstrating consistent treatment and legitimate injury actually strengthen most personal injury cases. The records that tend to create problems are incomplete records, records showing significant treatment gaps, or records that contradict what a patient reports. Our clinical team documents findings accurately and thoroughly, which protects both your health and your legal interests.

### What if my symptoms have already resolved — is it too late to seek care?

If your symptoms have fully resolved, you may not need ongoing treatment. However, if you sustained injuries in an accident and have not had a formal medical evaluation, it is still worth being seen. Some conditions — particularly disc injuries — can appear to improve only to recur or worsen later. A physician evaluation establishes the baseline of your condition, even if you are currently feeling better, and protects you if symptoms return.
