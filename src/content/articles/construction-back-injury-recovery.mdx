Back injuries are the most common workers' compensation claim in construction. A typical scenario: a framing carpenter lifts a bundle of shingles awkwardly, feels a sharp pull in the lower back, and powers through the shift. By morning, getting out of bed requires effort. What happens over the next six weeks will determine whether this worker returns to full duty or transitions into a chronic pain trajectory.

The timeline below is a representative composite based on common construction back injury presentations. It is not drawn from any individual patient. It is intended to illustrate what coordinated, evidence-based workers' compensation care looks like when it proceeds efficiently.

## Day 1 — ER, Imaging, and Acute Pain Control

The first priority is ruling out injuries that require immediate surgical intervention: fractures, cauda equina syndrome, or significant neurological compromise. An emergency department visit for a construction back injury typically includes:

- **History and physical.** Mechanism of injury, symptom onset, and a neurological screening exam assessing lower extremity strength, sensation, and reflexes.
- **X-rays.** Anterior-posterior and lateral lumbar spine films to evaluate bony structures. X-rays will not show disc or soft tissue injury, but they rule out fractures and assess alignment.
- **Initial work status.** Most acute back presentations leave the ED with a note for modified or restricted duty — not necessarily full work absence — and a referral for follow-up.
- **Acute pain management.** NSAIDs, muscle relaxants, or short-course oral steroids are commonly prescribed for acute musculoskeletal pain. Opioids are used conservatively, if at all, for acute presentations without clear neurological compromise.

The workers' comp carrier is notified on day one. An incident report is filed. This contemporaneous documentation is essential — it establishes the date and mechanism of injury in the medical record.

## Weeks 1–2 — Diagnosis Refinement and Pain Management Consult

The follow-up visit with a workers' compensation treating physician, ideally within 48–72 hours of the initial ED visit, is where the clinical picture sharpens. By this point, the pattern of pain — whether it's primarily axial (localized to the lower back), radicular (traveling into the leg), or both — is clearer, and the physician can make a more informed judgment about imaging.

**MRI of the lumbar spine** is typically ordered when symptoms suggest disc herniation or nerve root compression: leg pain following a dermatomal pattern, numbness, or weakness. In the absence of these red flags, guidelines recommend a conservative trial before advanced imaging. A skilled treating physician makes this call based on clinical findings, not reflexive protocol.

If the MRI reveals a disc herniation with nerve root involvement, a **pain management consult** is appropriate during this window. Options such as an epidural steroid injection can provide targeted anti-inflammatory relief that accelerates a return to function. Early pain management involvement is not an escalation — it's part of an efficient care pathway. Our [pain management team](/services/pain-management) works directly with workers' comp patients to coordinate this evaluation without delay.

Work status during weeks one and two is typically light or modified duty. For construction workers, modified duty can be challenging to accommodate on an active job site. The treating physician should document specific functional restrictions — lifting limits, postural requirements, restrictions on vibration exposure — that give the employer a clear picture of what the worker can safely perform.

## Weeks 2–4 — Active Physical Therapy and Modified Duty

Once the acute phase has resolved enough for the patient to tolerate active movement, [physical therapy](/services/physical-therapy) begins in earnest. This is the central phase of construction back injury recovery, and its outcome depends heavily on the quality and specificity of the PT program.

For a construction worker with lumbar disc pathology, an effective PT program typically includes:

- **Core stabilization training.** The deep stabilizers of the spine — transverse abdominis, multifidus — are often inhibited after acute injury. Retraining them reduces load on the passive structures (discs, facet joints) during work activities.
- **McKenzie or directional preference exercises.** For disc-related back pain, exercises performed in the direction of centralization (moving pain from the leg toward the midline) are both diagnostic and therapeutic.
- **Manual therapy.** Joint mobilization and soft tissue work address mobility restrictions and muscle guarding that develop in the acute phase.
- **Functional movement training.** Lifting mechanics, carrying posture, and the specific movement patterns of construction work are addressed explicitly — not just general strengthening.

A PT attendance expectation of two to three sessions per week during this phase is reasonable. Drop-off in attendance is a common point of failure. The treating physician and the physical therapist should communicate regularly about progress so that the work status note is updated in real time.

## Weeks 4–6 — Return-to-Work Conditioning

The final phase is a deliberate bridge between therapeutic exercise and the physical demands of returning to full construction duty. A construction worker's job is physically demanding — sustained forward bending, heavy lifting, working on uneven surfaces, overhead reach. Discharging a patient from PT when they can perform a clean deadlift in a clinic environment is not the same as clearing them to carry 80-pound bundles up a roof ladder.

A work conditioning or work hardening program addresses this gap. The PT progresses the patient through job-specific tasks at increasing loads, durations, and frequencies until they demonstrate the capacity to meet the physical demands of their position.

A **Functional Capacity Evaluation (FCE)** at the end of this phase provides an objective, documented assessment of the worker's capabilities. The FCE guides the treating physician in issuing a final return-to-work status — full duty, permanent restrictions, or maximum medical improvement — and supports any impairment rating the workers' comp case requires.

## Why Coordinated Multi-Specialty Care Cuts Recovery Time

The six-week timeline described above is achievable, but not automatic. It requires that every transition in the care pathway happens promptly: ER to follow-up within 48 hours, imaging read without delay, pain management scheduled in week two rather than week six, PT initiated while motivation is high and deconditioning is minimal.

When those transitions require navigating separate facilities, separate records systems, and separate scheduling queues, they slow down — adding weeks at every handoff.
