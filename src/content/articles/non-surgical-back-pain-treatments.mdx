If you have been dealing with back pain for weeks or months, the question of surgery has probably crossed your mind. Maybe a friend had spinal surgery. Maybe a doctor mentioned it as a future possibility. Maybe you are simply tired of hurting and wondering how far this needs to go before something actually works.

Here is what the evidence — and the clinical experience at **Rand Medical Center** in Arlington Heights, IL — consistently shows: the vast majority of people with chronic back pain respond well to non-surgical treatments. Surgery addresses a narrow set of spinal problems, and for most patients, the right combination of non-surgical interventions provides meaningful, lasting relief without the risks, costs, and recovery time that come with an operation.

The key word is "right combination." Not every back pain treatment works for every type of back pain. The source of your pain — whether it is a [herniated disc](/conditions/spine/herniated-disc) pressing on a nerve, [spinal stenosis](/conditions/spine/spinal-stenosis) narrowing the canal, arthritic facet joints, inflamed muscles, or some combination — determines which treatments are most likely to help you. That is why an accurate diagnosis is the foundation of every effective non-surgical treatment plan.

At Rand Medical Center, our pain management team treats hundreds of patients each year who find meaningful relief without ever needing an operation. Here are five of the most effective non-surgical approaches — what they are, how they work, and when they are most appropriate.

## 1. Physical Therapy and Targeted Exercise

[Physical therapy](/services/physical-therapy) is frequently the foundation of any effective back pain treatment plan, and for good reason. The spine is supported by an intricate network of muscles, ligaments, and tendons. When those supporting structures are weak, deconditioned, or imbalanced, painful spinal structures are subjected to forces they were not designed to bear. Physical therapy addresses this directly.

A skilled physical therapist begins with a detailed assessment of your movement patterns, strength, flexibility, and posture. They design a program specific to your diagnosis and functional deficits — not a generic set of stretches, but targeted exercises that strengthen the muscles most critical to your spinal stability, correct the movement dysfunctions contributing to your pain, and improve flexibility in the structures that have tightened around your injury.

### What physical therapy for back pain typically includes:

**Core stabilization exercises** — The deep core muscles, particularly the transverse abdominis and multifidus, function as a natural corset around the spine. Strengthening them reduces pressure on discs and facet joints during daily activities and movement.

**Hip and glute strengthening** — Weak hip musculature forces compensatory loading patterns that transfer excessive stress to the lumbar spine. Strengthening the gluteal muscles and hip abductors is often one of the highest-impact interventions for lower back pain, even when patients are surprised it is not focused directly on the back.

**Flexibility and mobility work** — Tight hip flexors, hamstrings, and thoracic spine limit normal movement and concentrate stress at the lumbar level. Systematic stretching and mobilization restore the full range of motion the spine needs to move without excessive strain at any single segment.

**Neuromuscular re-education** — Chronic pain alters the way the brain controls movement. Physical therapists use specific techniques to retrain the nervous system to activate muscles at the right time and in the right sequence, which reduces the risk of re-injury and improves long-term outcomes.

For many patients, consistent physical therapy over 6 to 12 weeks produces significant and lasting improvement, especially when combined with other treatments on this list. At Rand Medical Center, [physical therapy](/services/physical-therapy) is coordinated directly with pain management care — your therapist knows your diagnosis and what interventional procedures you have received, allowing them to progress your program in sync with your overall treatment plan.

## 2. Epidural Steroid Injections

[Epidural steroid injections](/services/pain-management/epidural-steroid-injections) are one of the most effective non-surgical interventions for back pain that is nerve-related — meaning pain caused by a disc, bone spur, or thickened ligament pressing on or irritating a spinal nerve.

The injection delivers a combination of corticosteroid and local anesthetic directly to the epidural space — the area surrounding the spinal cord and nerve roots inside the spinal canal. By placing anti-inflammatory medication precisely at the source of nerve irritation, the injection reduces swelling around the nerve far more effectively than oral anti-inflammatory medications that must travel through the entire bloodstream.

### Conditions that respond best to epidural steroid injections:

- [Sciatica](/conditions/spine/sciatica) — radiating pain traveling from the low back into the leg, caused by compression of the sciatic nerve or its roots
- [Herniated disc](/conditions/spine/herniated-disc) — when disc material presses on a nerve root, causing local and radiating pain
- [Spinal stenosis](/conditions/spine/spinal-stenosis) — narrowing of the spinal canal that compresses nerve roots, causing leg pain with walking (neurogenic claudication)
- [Degenerative disc disease](/conditions/spine/back-pain) with nerve involvement

The procedure is performed under **fluoroscopic (live X-ray) guidance** and takes approximately 15 to 30 minutes. Most patients go home the same day. The local anesthetic provides immediate but temporary relief; the corticosteroid takes 2 to 7 days to reach full effect and typically provides weeks to months of meaningful pain reduction.

Epidural steroid injections also serve a valuable **diagnostic purpose** — if the injection provides significant relief, it confirms which spinal level is generating your symptoms, guiding all subsequent treatment decisions. Learn more in our [complete guide to epidural steroid injections](/blog/epidural-steroid-injections-guide).

## 3. Facet Joint Injections and Radiofrequency Ablation

Not all back pain comes from discs and nerves. A substantial portion of chronic low back pain — estimates range from 15 to 45 percent — originates from the **facet joints**: small paired joints at each level of the spine that allow controlled movement between vertebrae. Facet joint pain is typically described as a dull, aching pain centered in the midline or just to the side of the spine, often worse with extension and rotation, and usually without significant radiation into the leg.

[Facet joint blocks](/services/pain-management/facet-joint-blocks) — also called medial branch blocks — serve both diagnostic and therapeutic purposes. A small amount of local anesthetic is injected near the medial branch nerves that carry sensation from the facet joints. If this injection provides significant relief, it confirms that the facet joints are generating your pain and identifies them as the appropriate treatment target.

### The two-step facet joint treatment approach:

**Step 1 — Diagnostic facet block:** Confirms the facet joints as the pain source and determines which spinal levels are involved.

**Step 2 — Radiofrequency ablation:** Once the pain source is confirmed, radiofrequency ablation uses carefully controlled heat energy to disrupt the medial branch nerves carrying pain signals from those joints. The disrupted nerves can no longer effectively transmit pain, providing relief that typically lasts **12 to 18 months or longer** — far exceeding what repeated steroid injections achieve.

This two-step approach is one of the most reliable and durable treatments available for arthritis-related back pain, [facet syndrome](/conditions/spine/facet-syndrome), and other facet-mediated conditions — all without surgery. When the nerves regenerate and pain returns, the procedure can be repeated.

## 4. Trigger Point Injections

Many patients with chronic back pain have a significant **muscle component** to their pain, even when structural problems like disc herniation also exist. Tight, painful muscle knots — known as trigger points — are extremely common in the muscles of the lower and mid back, and they refer pain to areas that can mimic disc or nerve pain closely enough to cause diagnostic confusion.

[Trigger point injections](/services/pain-management/trigger-point-injections) address this directly. A small amount of local anesthetic (sometimes combined with a low dose of corticosteroid) is injected precisely into the palpable knot within the muscle. The injection breaks the trigger point's self-sustaining contraction cycle, allowing the muscle to relax and the referred pain to subside.

### When trigger point injections are most appropriate:

- Back pain accompanied by clearly palpable, tender muscle knots
- [Myofascial pain syndrome](/conditions/muscle/myofascial-pain) — a condition characterized by widespread trigger points causing regional or referred pain
- Muscle spasm contributing to ongoing pain after disc injury or spine surgery
- Back pain that persists despite adequate treatment of underlying structural causes, suggesting a significant myofascial overlay

Trigger point injections often work best in combination with [physical therapy](/services/physical-therapy) — the injection resolves the acute trigger point, and therapeutic exercise prevents its recurrence by addressing the underlying muscle imbalances and movement dysfunctions that contributed to its development.

## 5. Medication Management and Lifestyle Modification

While medications alone rarely solve chronic back pain, they play an important supporting role in a comprehensive treatment plan. The key is selecting the right medications for your specific pain mechanism, using them at appropriate doses and durations, and monitoring for adverse effects — particularly with long-term use.

### Medications with evidence for chronic back pain:

**NSAIDs (non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs)** — Ibuprofen, naproxen, and prescription NSAIDs reduce both pain and inflammation. They are most effective for back pain with an inflammatory component and less useful for pure nerve pain.

**Muscle relaxants** — For back pain with significant muscle spasm, short-term muscle relaxants can reduce spasm-related pain, improve sleep, and make physical therapy more productive. They are generally not intended for long-term use.

**Neuropathic pain medications** — Medications like gabapentin and duloxetine specifically target nerve pain pathways and can be effective for [sciatica](/conditions/spine/sciatica), [neuropathy](/conditions/nerves/neuropathy), and other nerve-mediated back pain that does not respond well to anti-inflammatory medications.

**Topical medications** — Diclofenac gel, lidocaine patches, and capsaicin patches deliver medication locally with fewer systemic effects, making them excellent options for localized back pain.

### Lifestyle modifications that make a measurable difference:

**Weight management** — Excess body weight increases compressive load on spinal discs and facet joints. Even modest weight loss can reduce pain significantly in overweight patients with lumbar degeneration.

**Ergonomic optimization** — Prolonged sitting in poor posture is one of the most consistent aggravating factors for chronic back pain. Ergonomic chairs, proper desk height, and frequent position changes throughout the workday can dramatically reduce daily pain levels.

**Sleep quality** — Poor sleep amplifies pain perception and slows tissue healing. Addressing sleep hygiene, optimizing sleep position, and — when appropriate — treating underlying sleep disorders is a meaningful part of back pain management.

**Stress reduction** — Psychological stress significantly amplifies the brain's interpretation of pain signals. Mindfulness-based stress reduction, cognitive behavioral therapy, and other mind-body approaches have strong evidence for improving chronic pain outcomes.

**Activity modification** — The goal is not to avoid all activity, but to find the right activities. Low-impact exercise like walking, swimming, and cycling maintains spinal health, reduces inflammation, and improves mood without aggravating back structures.

## Combining Treatments: The Integrated Approach

One of the most important points in chronic back pain management is that these five treatments are not alternatives to each other — they are most powerful in combination. A patient with sciatica from a herniated disc might receive an epidural steroid injection to reduce nerve inflammation, followed immediately by physical therapy to strengthen the supporting muscles and correct movement dysfunctions, with carefully selected medications to manage breakthrough pain, and lifestyle modifications to prevent recurrence.

## Practical Tips for Managing Chronic Back Pain Day-to-Day

Between your appointments and procedures, these habits support your recovery:

- **Keep moving** — bed rest beyond 1 to 2 days for acute flares is counterproductive. Gentle movement maintains circulation and prevents the muscle atrophy that worsens pain.
- **Apply ice in the first 48 hours** of an acute flare, then transition to heat for chronic pain and muscle stiffness.
- **Track your pain** — a simple daily pain diary (0–10 scale, activities performed, sleep quality) helps your physician identify patterns and measure your response to treatments.
- **Do not skip physical therapy appointments** — consistency is the most important variable in therapy outcomes. Two sessions per week for 8 weeks is far more effective than sporadic attendance.
- **Communicate openly with your physician** about what is working and what is not. Pain management is iterative — the plan should evolve based on your response.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I know if my back pain requires surgery?

Surgery for back pain is typically considered only after **non-surgical treatments have been thoroughly tried and have failed** to provide adequate relief. The exceptions are emergencies: sudden loss of bladder or bowel control (cauda equina syndrome), rapidly progressive leg weakness, or fracture instability — these require urgent evaluation. For the vast majority of chronic back pain patients, the right sequence of non-surgical treatments provides sufficient relief to avoid surgery indefinitely.

### How long should I try non-surgical treatments before considering surgery?

There is no single answer, as it depends on your diagnosis, severity, and trajectory. Most spine surgeons recommend a **minimum of 6 to 12 weeks** of supervised non-surgical treatment before considering elective surgery for conditions like disc herniation or stenosis. Some patients improve dramatically within weeks; others take several months. The goal is meaningful functional improvement, not just waiting out a prescribed time period.

### Can physical therapy make back pain worse?

Occasionally, patients experience a temporary increase in soreness when beginning a new therapy program — similar to muscle soreness after any new physical activity. This is normal and usually resolves within a few days. True worsening — significantly increased pain, new neurological symptoms, or pain that persists beyond the first few sessions — should be reported to your physical therapist and physician immediately, as it may indicate a need to modify the program or reassess the diagnosis.

### Is it safe to exercise with a herniated disc?

In most cases, **yes — with appropriate guidance**. Exercise designed for disc conditions avoids positions and movements that increase disc pressure (such as flexion under load) and focuses on the stabilization and decompression movements that reduce nerve irritation. A physical therapist familiar with disc conditions can design a program that is both safe and effective. Avoiding all exercise with a herniated disc typically leads to worse outcomes over time.

## When to See a Specialist for Back Pain

If your back pain has lasted more than a few weeks, is getting progressively worse, or is affecting your ability to work, sleep, or function normally — it is time to see a pain management specialist. Early treatment consistently leads to better outcomes and reduces the risk of pain becoming chronic and deeply ingrained.

See a specialist promptly if pain is radiating into your leg, if you have numbness or tingling, if pain began after a [work injury](/conditions/other/workplace-injury) or [auto accident](/conditions/other/auto-injury), or if you have been told you may need surgery and want a thorough evaluation of non-surgical alternatives first.
