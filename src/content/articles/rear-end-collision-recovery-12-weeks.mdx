*What follows is a representative composite case based on the typical presentation and progression of rear-end whiplash at Rand Medical Center. It does not represent any specific patient. All details are generalized from clinical patterns, not individual records.*

---

Sarah, 38, was stopped at a red light on a suburban arterial road when the car behind her failed to brake. The impact was moderate—her car sustained a few thousand dollars of damage, the airbags did not deploy, and she drove away from the scene. She felt shaken but not seriously hurt. By the following morning, she could barely turn her head.

This is the most common presentation of **rear-end collision recovery** we see: a patient who left the scene believing the accident was minor, only to wake up the next day confronting a cervical spine that had a very different opinion.

## Week 1–2: The Acute Phase

Sarah came in on Day 2. Her cervical range of motion was reduced by approximately 40 percent in rotation and 30 percent in extension. Palpation revealed significant paraspinal muscle guarding from C2 through T2, with the most pronounced tenderness at the C5-C6 level. She reported an 8 out of 10 pain at rest, difficulty sleeping, and a headache that had been present since the morning after the accident.

Cervical X-rays showed no fracture and no instability. There was mild straightening of the cervical lordosis—consistent with acute muscle guarding—and minimal degenerative changes at C5-C6 that were pre-existing.

Her physician ordered physical therapy three times per week and prescribed a short course of anti-inflammatory medication. The first two therapy sessions focused on soft tissue mobilization to reduce guarding, application of therapeutic modalities for inflammation, and instruction in home exercises designed to maintain the range of motion that was still present.

By the end of Week 2, Sarah's pain had dropped to a 6. She was sleeping better—not well, but better. Rotation had improved marginally. The headaches were occurring daily but were less severe.

**Key lesson from this phase**: The goal of acute care is not resolution. It is stabilization—preventing the injury from becoming worse, interrupting the inflammation cycle, and establishing the foundation for progressive rehabilitation.

## Week 3–4: Imaging and PT Initiation

At the Week 3 follow-up, Sarah's neurological examination was clean—no upper extremity numbness, tingling, or weakness. Because her improvement was proceeding at a normal rate and her neurological screen was negative, MRI was not yet ordered. If neurological symptoms had emerged, or if she had not responded to conservative care by Week 4, MRI would have been the next step.

Physical therapy shifted from primarily passive care (hands-on manual therapy and modalities) to active participation. Sarah began:

- **Deep cervical flexor activation**: Low-load endurance exercises for the deep neck stabilizers, which had been reflexively inhibited since the accident
- **Scapular stabilization exercises**: Upper trapezius overactivation is almost universal after whiplash; specific exercises retrained lower trapezius and serratus anterior engagement
- **Cervical range-of-motion progression**: Active-assisted rotation and extension exercises to begin restoring the full arc of movement

Her pain at the Week 4 visit was a 4 out of 10 at rest, occasionally spiking to a 6 with sustained computer work. She had returned to driving short distances. Rotation was now at roughly 60 percent of expected normal.

**Key lesson from this phase**: Active participation in rehabilitation—not passive rest—is what advances recovery beyond the acute phase. Patients who continue passive care too long often plateau and develop chronic symptoms.

## Week 5–8: Active Rehabilitation

Weeks 5 through 8 represent the most intensive phase of whiplash rehabilitation and the period during which the largest functional gains typically occur.

Sarah's therapy program during this phase included:

- **Progressive cervical strengthening**: Resistance band exercises for cervical extensors and flexors, progressed weekly based on her tolerance
- **Postural retraining**: Sustained computer posture was identified as her primary aggravating factor. The therapy team addressed thoracic mobility, scapular positioning, and work station setup.
- **Proprioceptive training**: Gaze stability exercises and joint position sense tasks to address the cervicogenic component of her headaches
- **Cervicogenic headache treatment**: Targeted C1-C2 and C2-C3 joint mobilization by her therapist reduced her headache frequency from daily to two or three times per week by Week 7

By Week 8, Sarah reported pain of 2 to 3 at rest, with occasional spikes to 4 during prolonged activity. Cervical rotation was at 80 percent of normal. She was working full-time without significant functional limitation. Sleep had normalized.

At her Week 8 physician visit, imaging was reviewed. Given the absence of neurological symptoms and the functional progress, MRI was not indicated. She was progressing well within the normal recovery trajectory for moderate whiplash.

**Key lesson from this phase**: Measurable progress on objective metrics—not just pain reduction—is the reliable indicator of genuine recovery. Range of motion, strength measurements, and functional capacity tell the real story.

## Week 9–12: Return to Full Function

The final phase of recovery focused on consolidating gains, addressing any residual limitations, and verifying that Sarah met discharge criteria.

Remaining agenda items in Weeks 9 through 12:

- **Sustained-posture tolerance**: Could she work an eight-hour day at a computer without significant pain increase? By Week 10, yes.
- **Head-check range of motion**: Full rotational range restored to 95 percent of expected normal by Week 11—functionally adequate for safe driving.
- **Residual headache management**: Headaches dropped to once weekly by Week 11. This was attributed to a combination of cervical joint mobilization and a home exercise program she had maintained consistently.
- **Discharge with home program**: Sarah was formally discharged at Week 12 with a documented end-state—objective measurements recorded—and a home maintenance program.

Her final pain rating was 1 out of 10 at rest, 2 to 3 with vigorous activity. All neurological findings had been negative throughout. She returned to exercise including yoga, which she had avoided since the accident.

## What This Case Illustrates

**Recovery is nonlinear.** Weeks 3 and 4 felt slow to Sarah—there was improvement but not dramatic improvement. Week 6 felt like a turning point. Most patients experience a similar pattern of gradual change punctuated by more obvious functional milestones.

**Consistency matters more than intensity.** Sarah attended 89 percent of her scheduled therapy sessions. That attendance rate is directly correlated with her outcome. Patients who miss appointments frequently, or who stop therapy early because they feel "good enough," routinely plateau before they reach full function.

**"Better" is not the same as "recovered."** At Week 6, Sarah felt significantly better. She could have stopped therapy and declared herself fine. Instead, she continued through the full program and reached objectively normal cervical function. That difference—between functional adequacy and true recovery—is clinically and legally significant.

**Documentation built a complete case.** Sarah's medical record contained initial evaluation findings, objective measurements at each phase, imaging reports, therapy notes, and a formal discharge summary. When her attorney requested records to support her claim, the file was complete, specific, and internally consistent.
