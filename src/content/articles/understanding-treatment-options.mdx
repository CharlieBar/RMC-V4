When you are dealing with significant pain or an injury, it can feel like you are facing a stark choice: either live with it or have surgery. Many patients arrive at their first appointment believing these are their only two options. The reality is far more nuanced — and far more hopeful. Between "do nothing" and "have surgery" lies a wide spectrum of evidence-based treatments that help the majority of patients achieve meaningful relief without ever entering an operating room.

Understanding the full range of treatment options available to you — and how your physician determines which are right for your situation — empowers you to be an active participant in your care rather than a passive recipient of recommendations. At Rand Medical Center in Arlington Heights, IL, our physicians follow a systematic, stepped approach to treatment that starts with the least invasive options and advances only when clinically indicated.

This guide walks through each category of treatment, explains the conditions they address, and describes how decisions are made throughout the care process.

## How Your Physician Decides on a Treatment Plan

No two patients are alike, even when their diagnoses are identical. The treatment approach your physician recommends is based on a combination of factors unique to you:

**Your specific diagnosis:** A definitive diagnosis is the foundation of any treatment plan. The same symptom — lower back pain, for example — can arise from a herniated disc, spinal stenosis, sacroiliac joint dysfunction, muscular strain, or a combination of conditions. The appropriate treatment differs significantly depending on the source.

**Severity and duration:** An acute injury of three weeks' duration is managed differently from the same condition that has been present for three years and become chronic. Severity — how much the condition is affecting your function and quality of life — also influences how aggressively to pursue treatment.

**Your prior treatment history:** What has already been tried, and what has or hasn't worked, is essential context. A patient who has already completed 12 weeks of physical therapy without improvement is at a different point in the treatment algorithm than a patient who has not yet tried any conservative care.

**Your personal goals and preferences:** Some patients prioritize returning to high-level athletic activity; others simply want to be able to sleep through the night. Understanding your functional goals helps your physician calibrate the treatment plan to what matters most to you.

**Medical evidence:** Treatment recommendations at Rand Medical Center are grounded in current clinical evidence. When multiple approaches might be reasonable, we favor those with the strongest evidence base for your specific condition.

**Practical considerations:** Factors like your insurance coverage, the demands of your job, your ability to attend therapy sessions, and your tolerance for certain types of procedures are all relevant to building a realistic, achievable plan.

## Conservative Treatments: The Foundation of Care

For the majority of musculoskeletal and pain conditions, conservative treatment is the appropriate starting point — and for many patients, it produces outcomes that are comparable or superior to more invasive options.

### Physical Therapy

[Physical therapy](/services/physical-therapy) is among the most effective treatments available for a broad range of conditions, including [back pain](/conditions/spine/back-pain), [neck pain](/conditions/spine/neck-pain), [knee pain](/conditions/joints/knee-pain), shoulder injuries, and rehabilitation following procedures or surgery.

Physical therapy works through several mechanisms. **Therapeutic exercise** builds the strength and endurance of muscles that support injured or painful structures — reducing the load on damaged tissue and improving the body's ability to protect itself from re-injury. **Manual therapy** — hands-on techniques applied by a skilled therapist — can reduce pain, improve mobility, and break down scar tissue. **Neuromuscular re-education** retrains movement patterns that may be contributing to pain or putting abnormal stress on joints and soft tissue. **Patient education** teaches you to understand your condition and manage it independently over the long term.

A course of physical therapy typically involves two to three sessions per week for six to twelve weeks, combined with a daily home exercise program. The timeline varies based on your diagnosis and your response to treatment.

### Medication Management

Medications play a supporting role in most musculoskeletal treatment plans — rarely the primary treatment, but often valuable in reducing symptoms enough to allow you to participate in therapy and daily activities more effectively.

**Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)** — including both over-the-counter options and prescription-strength formulations — reduce inflammation and pain in musculoskeletal conditions. They are most effective when used consistently at appropriate doses for the prescribed duration rather than sporadically.

**Muscle relaxants** address the muscle spasm that often accompanies acute spinal injuries and soft tissue strains. They are typically used short-term.

**Neuropathic pain medications** — medications like gabapentin and duloxetine — are useful for nerve-related pain such as radiculopathy, neuropathy, and some chronic pain conditions. They work differently from standard pain relievers and may require several weeks at therapeutic dose before their full benefit is apparent.

**Topical medications** — anti-inflammatory creams, patches, or gels — can provide localized relief with minimal systemic effects, which is particularly valuable for patients who cannot tolerate oral medications well.

Your physician will select medications based on your diagnosis, your other medical conditions, and any medications you already take. Taking medications exactly as prescribed — including completing the full course — is important for achieving the intended benefit.

### Activity Modification and Ergonomics

Modifying how you move, work, and perform daily activities is a foundational component of treatment that is frequently underestimated. For patients with [back pain](/conditions/spine/back-pain) related to prolonged sitting, or [arthritis](/conditions/joints/arthritis) aggravated by repetitive joint loading, changes to daily habits and work ergonomics can reduce pain significantly without any other intervention.

Activity modification does not mean stopping all activity — in most cases, appropriate movement is beneficial and complete rest is counterproductive. It means adjusting the activities that are aggravating your condition while maintaining the activities that support your recovery.

## Interventional Procedures: When Conservative Care Isn't Enough

When conservative treatments have been tried appropriately and have not provided adequate relief — or when a patient's pain is severe enough that it is preventing them from engaging with physical therapy — interventional procedures are the next step. These are minimally invasive treatments that are more targeted than medication but less invasive than surgery.

### Epidural Steroid Injections

[Epidural steroid injections](/services/pain-management/epidural-steroid-injections) deliver corticosteroid medication directly to the epidural space surrounding the spinal cord and nerve roots. They are most commonly used for radicular pain — the shooting, burning, or electric pain that travels from the spine into the arm or leg, often caused by a herniated disc or spinal stenosis compressing a nerve.

By delivering anti-inflammatory medication precisely to the site of nerve compression, epidural injections can dramatically reduce radicular pain, often providing relief for weeks to months. This window of reduced pain is typically used to intensify participation in physical therapy — the combination of injection and therapy is more effective than either alone.

[Transforaminal epidural injections](/services/pain-management/transforaminal-epidural-injections) and [selective nerve root blocks](/services/pain-management/selective-nerve-root-blocks) are variations that target specific nerve roots more precisely and can also serve a diagnostic function — helping confirm which specific nerve is the pain generator.

### Joint Injections

[Joint injections](/services/pain-management/joint-injections) deliver corticosteroid medication directly into a painful joint — most commonly the knee, hip, shoulder, or facet joints of the spine. For joints affected by [arthritis](/conditions/joints/arthritis), inflammation, or injury, these injections can significantly reduce pain and improve function.

Joint injections are particularly valuable when a patient has not responded adequately to oral anti-inflammatory medications, when they cannot tolerate those medications, or when more targeted relief is needed to allow physical therapy to proceed.

[Sacroiliac joint injections](/services/pain-management/sacroiliac-joint-injections) specifically address the SI joint — a common and frequently overlooked source of low back and buttock pain, particularly in patients who have already undergone lumbar spine treatment without relief.

[Bursa injections](/services/pain-management/bursa-injections) target inflamed bursae — small fluid-filled sacs that cushion tendons and bones — which are a common source of pain in the shoulder, hip, and knee.

### Nerve Blocks

[Nerve blocks](/services/pain-management/nerve-blocks) interrupt pain signal transmission from specific nerves using local anesthetic, sometimes combined with corticosteroid medication. They can provide rapid, targeted relief for localized pain conditions and serve both therapeutic and diagnostic purposes.

[Facet joint blocks](/services/pain-management/facet-joint-blocks) target the small joints along the sides of the spine that are frequent sources of axial (non-radiating) back and neck pain, particularly in patients with degenerative changes or arthritis in those joints.

[Trigger point injections](/services/pain-management/trigger-point-injections) address localized areas of muscle tightness — trigger points — that generate pain both locally and referred to distant sites. They are commonly used in patients with myofascial pain and muscle spasm.

### Radiofrequency Ablation

[Radiofrequency ablation (RFA)](/services/pain-management/radiofrequency-ablation) uses precisely applied heat energy to disrupt the function of the sensory nerves that transmit pain signals from specific structures — most commonly the facet joints of the spine. Unlike injections, which provide temporary relief, RFA can provide sustained pain relief lasting 12 to 24 months or longer, because it works by interrupting the neural pathway rather than simply reducing inflammation.

RFA is typically considered after a patient has had a positive response to diagnostic nerve blocks targeting the same nerves — confirming that those nerves are the pain source before proceeding to the longer-duration treatment.

### Spinal Cord Stimulation

[Spinal cord stimulation](/services/pain-management/spinal-cord-stimulation) is an advanced interventional option for patients with chronic, refractory pain who have not responded to multiple other treatments. A small device delivers electrical impulses to the spinal cord that modulate pain signal transmission, significantly reducing the perception of pain. It is most commonly used for complex regional pain syndrome, failed back surgery syndrome, and severe neuropathic pain.

## Surgical Options: When They Become Appropriate

Surgery is appropriate for a specific subset of patients whose conditions have not responded to well-implemented conservative and interventional treatment, or whose diagnoses involve structural problems that cannot be resolved without surgical correction. At Rand Medical Center, we take a conservative approach to surgical referral — surgery is recommended when the evidence supports it for a specific indication, not as a default for persistent pain.

### Arthroscopic Surgery

[Arthroscopic surgery](/services/orthopedics/arthroscopic-surgery) uses small incisions and a camera to address problems inside joints — torn ligaments, damaged cartilage, loose bodies, and other structural issues in the knee, shoulder, hip, and other joints. Arthroscopic procedures have significantly shorter recovery times than open surgery and are appropriate for a range of joint injuries and degenerative conditions.

### Spine Surgery

[Spine surgery](/services/orthopedics/spine-surgery) addresses structural spinal problems — herniated discs causing severe neurological compromise, spinal stenosis that has not responded to non-surgical management, or spinal instability requiring fusion. The indications for spine surgery are specific, and surgery is recommended only when the structural problem matches the clinical symptoms and conservative care has been appropriately exhausted.

### Joint Replacement

[Joint replacement](/services/orthopedics/joint-replacement) — most commonly of the knee or hip — is appropriate for patients with advanced arthritis that has destroyed joint cartilage and causes severe pain and functional limitation that has not been adequately managed with conservative measures. Modern joint replacement surgery produces excellent outcomes in appropriately selected patients, with the majority reporting dramatically improved pain and function.

## The Stepped Care Approach at Rand Medical Center

Our clinical approach follows a principle of **stepped care** — beginning with the least invasive options and advancing to more intensive treatments based on your response. This approach:

- Minimizes exposure to the risks inherent in more invasive treatments
- Confirms the diagnosis through response to treatment
- Maximizes the benefit of each treatment level before moving on
- Allows you and your physician to make informed decisions together based on demonstrated response

This does not mean every patient must complete 12 weeks of physical therapy before receiving an injection, or that surgery is always a last resort after years of failed treatment. There are clinical situations where more aggressive early intervention is clearly indicated — severe nerve compression with neurological deficit, for example, or a fracture requiring surgical stabilization. The stepped approach is a framework, not a rigid protocol, and your physician applies clinical judgment to your specific situation.

## Asking Informed Questions About Your Treatment Options

Being an informed patient is an important part of getting good outcomes. When your physician presents treatment options, these questions help you make better decisions:

- What is the evidence that this treatment works for my specific condition?
- What are the expected benefits, and how long should they last?
- What are the risks, and how common are they?
- What happens if this treatment doesn't work — what is the next step?
- Are there alternative approaches, and how do they compare?
- What can I do on my own — exercises, activity modification — to support this treatment?

Your physician at Rand Medical Center will always be willing to discuss these questions. Shared decision-making — where your preferences and values are incorporated into the treatment plan alongside clinical evidence — produces better outcomes and greater satisfaction with care.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I know if I need surgery or if something less invasive will work?

The answer depends on your specific diagnosis and clinical picture. Most musculoskeletal and pain conditions respond well to conservative and interventional treatment, and surgery is not the first-line recommendation for most patients. However, some conditions — severe structural problems, significant neurological compromise, advanced joint destruction — may have surgery as the most appropriate or most expedient treatment. Your physician will discuss where your condition falls in that spectrum and explain the rationale for their recommendation.

### What if I want to try physical therapy before considering injections?

Physical therapy is almost always a reasonable first approach for musculoskeletal and pain conditions, and your physician will typically recommend it as part of any initial treatment plan. If you have concerns about injections or other interventional procedures, communicate those preferences to your physician. They will work with you to develop a plan consistent with your preferences while making sure you understand the clinical tradeoffs.

### How long does it typically take to see results from treatment?

This varies widely by condition, treatment type, and individual patient response. Physical therapy for an acute injury may produce noticeable improvement within two to four weeks. Chronic conditions typically require longer treatment timelines. Medications may take two to six weeks to reach their full effect. Injections often provide initial relief within several days to two weeks. Your physician will set realistic expectations for your specific treatment at your visit.

### What if a treatment stops working?

This is a common and normal part of managing musculoskeletal and pain conditions over time. Injections may need to be repeated, medications may need to be adjusted, and surgical options become more relevant as conditions progress. The important thing is to stay engaged with your care team and communicate changes in your symptoms promptly. Our [pain management](/services/pain-management) team is experienced at managing complex, evolving conditions over the long term.
