If you've ever waded into a warm pool and felt immediate relief from the pressure that weighs on your joints throughout the day, you already understand the core principle of aquatic therapy. That sensation — of your body becoming lighter, your muscles relaxing, and pain stepping back — is not incidental. It is the mechanism that makes hydrotherapy one of the most powerful and underutilized tools in physical rehabilitation.

For patients in Arlington Heights, IL dealing with [arthritis](/conditions/joints/arthritis), chronic [back pain](/conditions/spine/back-pain), post-surgical stiffness, or neurological conditions, aquatic therapy can offer something land-based exercise cannot: the ability to move, strengthen, and heal without the punishing load of full body weight. At **Rand Medical Center**, our licensed physical therapists use evidence-based aquatic protocols to help patients rebuild function when traditional exercise is too painful or simply not yet possible.

This article explains the science behind aquatic therapy, what a typical session looks like, who benefits most, and how to know whether it's the right step for your recovery.

## Why Water Works: The Physics Behind Aquatic Therapy

The therapeutic value of water is rooted in four fundamental properties: **buoyancy**, **resistance**, **hydrostatic pressure**, and **thermodynamics**. Understanding these helps explain why aquatic therapy consistently produces outcomes that land-based therapy alone cannot achieve for certain populations.

**Buoyancy** is the property most patients experience first. When you are submerged to your waist in water, buoyancy reduces the effective weight your joints carry by approximately **50 percent**. Submerged to the chest, that offloading increases to roughly **75 percent**. At neck depth, joints bear as little as **10 percent** of your normal body weight. For a 180-pound person with severe knee [osteoarthritis](/conditions/joints/arthritis), that means exercising at a functional load of just 18 pounds — enough to build strength without driving inflammatory flares.

**Resistance** is the counterbalancing force that makes aquatic therapy more than just rest. Water is approximately 800 times denser than air, which means every movement you make in a pool — a leg raise, a walking step, an arm swing — is met with gentle, uniform resistance in all directions. This multidirectional resistance strengthens muscles more comprehensively than most gym machines, which typically work muscles along a single plane.

**Hydrostatic pressure** refers to the pressure water exerts on all submerged surfaces of the body. This pressure, which increases with depth, improves circulation and venous return, reduces swelling in injured limbs, and provides sensory feedback to joints that may have lost proprioception — the body's awareness of its own position in space. For patients recovering from ankle sprains or knee surgeries, this proprioceptive feedback is a critical component of safe rehabilitation.

**Warm water temperature** (therapy pools are typically maintained between 92°F and 96°F) promotes muscle relaxation, reduces pain signal transmission, and increases tissue extensibility — making joints more receptive to range-of-motion work. The warmth is therapeutic in its own right, which is why many patients report leaving aquatic sessions feeling more relief than they've experienced in weeks.

## What Aquatic Therapy Sessions Look Like

A common misconception is that aquatic therapy simply means swimming laps. In reality, most patients in aquatic therapy cannot and should not swim — at least not at first. Therapeutic pool sessions are **structured, progressive, and therapist-guided**, with exercises chosen specifically for your diagnosis and goals.

At Rand Medical Center, your aquatic therapy session begins with a review of your progress since the last visit and any changes in pain or function. Your therapist then guides you through a sequence that typically includes:

**Warm-up and gait training.** For many patients, simply walking in chest-depth water is the first milestone. The buoyancy deloads the spine and lower extremity joints while the resistance challenges balance and strengthens the hip flexors and core. Therapists may progress patients to sideways walking, backward walking, or high-knee marching as mobility improves.

**Range-of-motion exercises.** The warmth and buoyancy of the pool create ideal conditions for regaining joint mobility. Hip circles, shoulder rolls, ankle pumps, and gentle lumbar rotation are performed more comfortably in water than on a treatment table, especially in the early weeks after surgery or during an inflammatory flare.

**Strengthening exercises.** Using foam noodles, resistance paddles, or simply the water itself, patients perform squats, leg presses against the pool wall, hip abduction exercises, bicep curls, and shoulder raises. The resistance level can be adjusted instantly by changing the speed of movement — moving faster increases resistance, moving slower decreases it — giving the therapist precise control over intensity.

**Balance and proprioception training.** Standing on one leg, stepping over objects on the pool floor, or performing single-leg exercises challenges the neuromuscular system in a safe environment where falls are cushioned and the fear of injury is reduced. This is particularly valuable for older adults working to prevent falls or patients rebuilding confidence after injury.

**Cool-down and stretching.** The session concludes with gentle stretching and breathing exercises. Many patients report that stretches performed in warm water achieve a greater range than the same stretches on land, allowing the therapist to make meaningful progress on flexibility goals within fewer sessions.

Sessions typically last 45 to 60 minutes, and patients are encouraged to voice their pain levels throughout. A good aquatic therapy session should never push you past a **4 out of 10 on the pain scale**.

## Who Benefits Most From Aquatic Therapy

Aquatic therapy is not just for one type of patient — it is a versatile modality that serves a wide range of conditions across the age spectrum. That said, certain populations tend to see the most dramatic benefits.

**Patients with arthritis** — both rheumatoid and osteoarthritis — often find that land-based exercise triggers painful flares that set back their progress. The deloaded environment of the pool allows them to maintain and build strength without inflammatory consequences. Multiple clinical trials have demonstrated significant reductions in joint pain and improvements in physical function for arthritis patients following aquatic therapy programs.

**Post-surgical patients** recovering from [joint replacement](/services/orthopedics), ACL reconstruction, rotator cuff repair, or spinal decompression can begin aquatic therapy earlier than land-based exercise — sometimes within days of surgery once the incision is healed and waterproofed. Early mobility is critical for preventing scar tissue formation and regaining range of motion, and the pool provides the perfect low-load environment to begin that process.

**Patients with chronic [back pain](/conditions/spine/back-pain) or [sciatica](/conditions/spine/sciatica)** benefit from the spinal decompression that occurs naturally when buoyancy reduces axial loading. Many patients with disc-related pain or spinal stenosis who cannot tolerate standing or walking for more than a few minutes find they can exercise comfortably in the pool for 45 minutes or more.

**Older adults** dealing with balance deficits, generalized weakness, and the fear of falling represent another ideal aquatic therapy population. The water's cushioning effect removes the psychological barrier of exercise anxiety, allowing patients to push harder and progress faster than they would in a traditional gym setting.

**Patients with neurological conditions** including stroke, multiple sclerosis, and Parkinson's disease benefit from the warmth, resistance, and proprioceptive feedback of the water environment. Aquatic therapy supports gait retraining and coordination work in a forgiving environment that reduces injury risk.

**Deconditioned patients** — those who have been inactive due to illness, surgery, or severe pain — often find that the pool is the only place they can exercise without immediately reaching their pain or fatigue limits. Aquatic therapy builds the baseline fitness that makes land-based exercise possible again.

## Aquatic vs. Land-Based Therapy: How They Work Together

It is important to understand that aquatic therapy is rarely a permanent substitute for land-based physical therapy — it is most often a **bridge** that enables land-based work to begin sooner and progress more safely. The two approaches are complementary, not competing.

For a patient who has just undergone [knee replacement surgery](/services/orthopedics), aquatic therapy might begin in weeks two through four, when walking on land is still difficult and swelling is pronounced. By week six or eight, as strength and range of motion improve in the pool, the therapist begins integrating land-based exercises — first on soft surfaces, then on standard gym equipment, then on sport- or work-specific activities. The aquatic sessions taper as the land-based program expands.

This progression matters because **full rehabilitation ultimately requires weight-bearing**. Bones, tendons, ligaments, and cartilage adapt to load — they need the stimulus of gravity and body weight to remodel correctly. Aquatic therapy accelerates the early phases of recovery so that patients can reach full weight-bearing with less pain, less fear, and more functional muscle strength.

At Rand Medical Center, our physical therapists assess each patient's readiness to transition regularly, so there is no unnecessary prolonging of aquatic-only care once you are ready for the next level.

## What to Expect Before, During, and After Your First Session

**Before your session:** Wear a well-fitting swimsuit that allows free movement. If you have a recent surgical incision, your therapist will verify that it is fully closed and waterproofed with an appropriate dressing before you enter the pool. Bring a towel, flip-flops for the pool deck, and a change of clothes. Avoid eating a large meal within 90 minutes of your session.

**During your first session:** Your therapist will introduce you to the pool environment gradually. If you are uncomfortable in water or have not swum in years, say so — aquatic therapy does not require swimming ability and can be conducted entirely in shallow water with a therapist beside you. You will work through a gentle initial protocol designed more for assessment than intensity.

**After your session:** It is normal to feel mild muscle fatigue, similar to a light workout. Most patients also feel a reduction in pain for several hours following the warmth and movement of the session. Drink water to rehydrate, and note any new pain or unusual symptoms to report at your next visit.

## Practical Advice: Preparing for Aquatic Therapy Success

Getting the most out of your aquatic therapy program requires active engagement both in the pool and between sessions. Here is what works:

**Show up consistently.** The frequency of sessions — typically two to three times per week — is designed to produce a cumulative adaptation response. Missing sessions disrupts the progression and slows recovery.

**Do your home exercises.** Your therapist will prescribe land-based exercises between aquatic sessions. These typically include gentle range-of-motion work, core activation, and balance exercises. Completing these daily maintains the gains made in the pool and accelerates your overall progress.

**Communicate pain levels honestly.** If an exercise causes a sharp or new type of pain, tell your therapist immediately. Aquatic therapy should produce mild exertion and fatigue — not new injury. Adjustments to your program can be made on the spot.

**Track your progress.** Keep a simple log of what exercises you did, how many repetitions, and your pain level before and after. This record helps your therapist make precise adjustments and gives you tangible evidence of your improvement.

**Stay warm after sessions.** Leaving a heated pool and heading into cold air can cause muscle tension. Dry off quickly, dress warmly, and allow yourself a brief rest before driving if you feel fatigued.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is aquatic therapy covered by insurance?

In most cases, yes. Aquatic therapy is a form of [physical therapy](/services/physical-therapy) and is covered under the same physical therapy benefits as land-based treatment when medically necessary. Coverage depends on your specific insurance plan and diagnosis. The team at **Rand Medical Center** can verify your benefits before your first visit and help you understand your out-of-pocket costs.

### Do I need to know how to swim?

No. Aquatic therapy is not swimming and does not require swimming ability. Sessions are conducted in shallow to mid-depth water, and your therapist is with you throughout. Pool lifts and hydraulic chairs are available for patients who have difficulty entering and exiting the pool independently.

### How many sessions will I need?

The number of sessions depends on your condition, severity, and goals. Some patients see significant improvement in 6 to 8 sessions. Others with complex post-surgical rehab or chronic conditions may continue for 12 to 20 sessions before transitioning to land-based exercise. Your therapist will give you an estimated timeline at your initial evaluation and update it as you progress.

### Can aquatic therapy make my condition worse?

Aquatic therapy is generally very safe when conducted by a licensed physical therapist. Contraindications include open wounds that cannot be waterproofed, active urinary tract infections, uncontrolled bowel incontinence, and severe cardiovascular instability. Your therapist will screen you for these before recommending pool therapy.

## When Aquatic Therapy Isn't Enough: Signs You Need Additional Evaluation

Aquatic therapy is highly effective, but it is not a standalone treatment for all conditions. Watch for these signs that your recovery may need additional medical attention:

- **Pain that is not improving** or is getting worse after 4 to 6 sessions despite good adherence
- **New neurological symptoms** such as numbness, tingling, or weakness in your arms or legs
- **Sudden increase in swelling** around a joint or surgical site
- **Fever, redness, or warmth** at a surgical incision, which may indicate infection
- **Loss of bladder or bowel control**, which requires immediate medical evaluation

If any of these occur, contact your care team promptly.
