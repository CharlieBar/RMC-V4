Walking into a new medical practice for the first time involves a certain amount of uncertainty — especially when you are dealing with pain, a recent injury, or a condition that has been affecting your quality of life. You want to communicate your situation clearly, understand your options, and leave with a plan. But it is easy to forget important details in the moment, or to realize afterward that you left out something your doctor really needed to know.

A well-prepared first appointment is a better appointment. Your physician can get to the important clinical questions faster, your history is documented more completely, and the plan that comes out of that visit reflects a fuller picture of your situation. At Rand Medical Center in Arlington Heights, IL, our team sees patients for a wide range of conditions — from acute injuries to chronic pain to occupational health — and the patients who come prepared consistently get more out of their first visit.

This guide covers everything you need to know before arriving: what to bring, how to prepare your health history, what to expect during the visit, and how to make the most of the time with your physician.

## What to Bring to Your First Appointment

Gathering the right documents and information before your visit saves time and ensures your physician has the full context needed to evaluate your situation accurately.

### Identification and Insurance

- **Valid government-issued photo ID** (driver's license, state ID, or passport)
- **Insurance card** — bring the physical card rather than relying on a photo, as our billing team may need to scan both the front and back
- **Secondary insurance card** if you have more than one plan
- **Workers' comp claim number** and insurer information if your visit relates to a work injury
- **Referral from your primary care physician** if your insurance plan requires one (common with HMO plans)

If you are unsure what your insurance requires or whether Rand Medical Center is in-network for your plan, call [(224) 735-3522](tel:+12247353522) before your visit and our billing team will verify your coverage. You can also review our [insurance information](/patient-resources/insurance) for more guidance.

### Medication Information

Bring a complete, current list of every medication you take, including:

- **Prescription medications** — name, dosage, and frequency
- **Over-the-counter medications** — pain relievers, antihistamines, supplements, vitamins, or other products you take regularly
- **Topical medications** — patches, creams, or gels

If you take your medications in the morning, consider bringing the actual bottles to your appointment rather than a written list — this eliminates transcription errors and ensures your physician has the exact formulations. For patients managing multiple medications, a pharmacy-printed medication list is also useful.

Be prepared to discuss any medications you have **tried and stopped**, particularly for pain management. If you took a certain anti-inflammatory and it didn't help, or if you had a reaction to a medication, that information directly affects what your physician will and won't recommend.

### Prior Imaging and Medical Records

Bringing any existing imaging significantly improves the quality of your initial evaluation. Physicians can make much more informed decisions when they can review actual images rather than relying solely on a written report.

- **X-rays** — if available on a disc or accessible through a patient portal
- **MRI or CT scans** — particularly relevant for spinal, joint, and soft tissue conditions
- **Bone density scans or other specialized imaging**
- **Radiology reports** — even if you cannot bring the images themselves, the written report from a radiologist is useful

If your imaging is stored in a previous provider's patient portal, log in before your appointment and determine whether you can download images or share access. Many portals allow you to generate a temporary access code for another provider.

Also bring **records from previous providers** who have treated the same condition or injury — particularly any prior specialist notes, surgical reports, or therapy discharge summaries. For patients transferring care for a chronic condition, a summary from your previous physician is extremely helpful.

### Accident and Injury Documentation

If your visit is related to an accident or injury:

- **Police report** or incident report number for auto accidents or workplace incidents
- **Date, time, and circumstances** of the injury written out clearly — in the stress of a first appointment, it is easy to omit important details
- **Name and contact information for your personal injury attorney**, if you have retained one
- **Workers' compensation claim information** including the name of your employer's workers' comp carrier and your adjuster's contact information

For auto accident and personal injury cases, see our [injury services](/services/injury-services) page for information on how we work with these cases.

## How to Prepare Your Health History

Your physician will ask about your medical history, but the quality of your answers affects the quality of the evaluation. Taking 15 to 20 minutes before your appointment to think through and write down the following will make a significant difference.

### Your Current Symptoms

Think carefully about your primary complaint — the main reason you are coming in — and prepare to describe it precisely:

**Location:** Where exactly is the pain or symptom? Does it stay in one place or does it radiate or move to other areas? For example, low back pain that radiates down the leg has different clinical implications than back pain that stays localized.

**Character:** What does it feel like? Sharp, dull, aching, burning, stabbing, throbbing, pressure? Understanding the character of your pain helps narrow the diagnosis.

**Severity:** On a scale of 0 to 10, where 0 is no pain and 10 is the worst pain imaginable, how would you rate your typical daily pain? Your pain at its worst? Your pain at its best?

**Timing:** Is the pain constant or intermittent? If intermittent, how long do episodes last and how often do they occur? Is it worse at particular times of day, such as in the morning or after long periods of sitting?

**Aggravating and relieving factors:** What makes it worse? What makes it better? Specific activities, positions, rest, ice, heat, or medications?

**Functional impact:** How is your symptom affecting your daily life? Can you do your job? Sleep through the night? Participate in activities you normally enjoy? Be specific — this information is relevant to your care plan and to any disability or workers' comp documentation.

**Onset:** When did this start? Did it start suddenly (from a specific incident) or gradually? If from an incident, describe what happened.

### Your Relevant Medical History

- **Prior injuries** to the same body part or region — even injuries that seemed to resolve at the time can be relevant
- **Prior surgeries** — type, date, and which facility
- **Chronic medical conditions** (diabetes, hypertension, heart disease, autoimmune conditions, etc.)
- **Known allergies** — particularly to medications, latex, or contrast dye used in imaging
- **Family history** relevant to your current condition — arthritis, osteoporosis, and many other conditions have genetic components

### Your List of Questions

Write down the questions you want to ask your physician before you go. It is easy to walk out of an appointment and remember three questions you forgot to ask. Common important questions include:

- What do you think is causing my symptoms?
- What tests or imaging do I need?
- What are my treatment options?
- What is the expected timeline for recovery or improvement?
- Are there activity restrictions I should follow?
- What should I do if my symptoms worsen before my next appointment?

Bring this list with you and don't hesitate to work through it systematically during the visit.

## What to Expect During Your First Visit

Understanding the structure of your first appointment helps manage expectations and reduces anxiety about the unknown.

### Check-In and Paperwork

Arrive 15 minutes early to allow time for check-in, insurance verification, and any remaining paperwork. You will be asked to present your photo ID and insurance card at the front desk.

### Vital Signs and Initial Assessment

A medical assistant or nurse will typically take your vital signs — blood pressure, pulse, temperature, and weight — before you see the physician. They may also ask preliminary questions about your chief complaint and current medications to ensure the chart is complete before the doctor enters.

### Physician Evaluation

Your physician will conduct a thorough evaluation that typically includes:

**History taking:** A detailed discussion of your symptoms, medical history, and the circumstances of any injury. This is the most important part of the visit — your physician is building a complete clinical picture. Be as specific and detailed as possible.

**Physical examination:** A focused physical exam relevant to your complaint. For musculoskeletal and pain conditions, this typically includes assessment of range of motion, strength, reflexes, sensation, and specific orthopedic tests designed to identify the source of your symptoms.

**Review of imaging or records:** If you have brought prior imaging or medical records, your physician will review them during or after the exam and incorporate findings into their assessment.

**Assessment and diagnosis:** Based on the history and examination, your physician will share their preliminary assessment — their best clinical understanding of what is causing your symptoms. In some cases a definitive diagnosis requires imaging or further testing, and your physician will explain what they need and why.

**Treatment plan discussion:** Your physician will outline their recommended approach, which may include ordering additional imaging or lab work, prescribing medication, initiating [physical therapy](/services/physical-therapy), recommending an interventional procedure, or referring you to a specialist within our practice. They will explain the rationale for each recommendation.

A first appointment for a new complex condition typically takes **45 to 60 minutes**. Visits for more focused concerns may be shorter.

### Imaging and Diagnostics

If your physician orders X-rays or other diagnostic studies that can be performed at our Arlington Heights facility, these may be completed the same day. Our [diagnostic services](/services/diagnostic-surgical-services) include on-site X-ray capability and referral coordination for MRI and advanced imaging.

Having imaging done on the same day as your evaluation can significantly accelerate the diagnostic process and allow your physician to review results at a follow-up visit rather than waiting through a separate appointment cycle.

## After Your Appointment: What Comes Next

Leave your first appointment with a clear understanding of the following:

- **Your preliminary diagnosis or working diagnosis**, and what additional testing is needed to confirm it
- **Your next steps** — scheduled follow-up, imaging to complete, prescriptions to fill, exercises to begin
- **Your restrictions**, if any — activity limitations, work restrictions, lifting limits
- **Who to call** if your symptoms worsen or you have questions before your next appointment

If you leave uncertain about any of these, call us at [(224) 735-3522](tel:+12247353522). It is far better to clarify early than to wait until your next appointment with unresolved uncertainty.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I stop taking my current medications before my first visit?

Do not change your medications before your first appointment unless advised to do so by a physician. Continue taking what you currently take, bring your complete medication list, and discuss your medications with your new physician at the visit. They will advise on any adjustments at that point.

### What if I don't have all my prior records or imaging?

Come anyway. Prior records are helpful but not required to begin your evaluation. Your physician will conduct a thorough history and examination regardless of what records are available. Missing records can often be requested from previous providers after your visit. If your prior imaging was done at another facility, we can provide you with a records request authorization to send to them.

### How long will my first appointment take?

A first appointment for a new, complex condition typically takes 45 to 60 minutes. Allow up to 90 minutes to be safe, particularly if imaging needs to be completed on the same day. If you have a time constraint, let the front desk know when you check in.

### What if I need to bring a family member or caregiver?

Family members and caregivers are welcome. Having a support person with you can be helpful — they may remember details you forget and can help you recall the physician's recommendations afterward. Please note that in some circumstances, the physician may ask to speak with you privately as part of the evaluation.
