Spinal cord stimulation is not a first-line treatment. By the time a pain physician raises the option, a patient has typically traveled a long road: conservative measures, physical therapy, medications, and interventional procedures such as injections or nerve blocks — each providing partial or temporary benefit, but not durable relief. If that arc sounds familiar, it may be time to understand what SCS offers and whether you are a realistic candidate.

## How Spinal Cord Stimulation Works

Spinal cord stimulation is a form of neuromodulation — it changes how the nervous system processes pain signals rather than targeting the anatomical source of pain directly. A small device, similar in concept to a cardiac pacemaker, delivers mild electrical pulses to the spinal cord via thin leads (wires) positioned in the epidural space.

These electrical pulses interfere with pain signals traveling up the spinal cord to the brain. Depending on the device and programming settings, the result is either a replacement sensation — classically a mild tingling called paresthesia — or, with newer high-frequency and burst SCS systems, simply an absence of the previous pain without any noticeable sensation at all. Modern SCS technology has advanced considerably; many patients now use high-frequency systems that provide pain relief without any paresthesia.

The mechanism is not tissue destruction or nerve ablation. The spinal cord and surrounding nerves remain intact. The device simply modulates the signal. This reversibility is one of SCS's defining advantages: if the device is turned off, the underlying pain state returns to baseline. Nothing has been permanently altered.

SCS is used most commonly for [neuropathy](/conditions/nerves/neuropathy), failed back surgery syndrome, complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS), and chronic [sciatica](/conditions/spine/sciatica) that has not resolved with other treatment. It is also employed in certain cases of refractory angina and peripheral vascular disease, though those applications are outside the scope of most musculoskeletal pain practices.

## Who's a Candidate (and Who Isn't)

Patient selection is the most important variable in SCS outcomes. The procedure performs best when applied to the right patient.

**Factors that support candidacy:**
- Chronic neuropathic pain (burning, shooting, electrical-quality pain) that has lasted six months or more
- Failure of at least two to three conservative and interventional treatments
- Pain is primarily neuropathic in character — SCS is less effective for purely mechanical or structural pain
- No untreated psychological condition that would interfere with patient-reported outcomes
- Patient is motivated, realistic about expectations, and committed to the follow-up care that SCS requires
- No active implanted devices (certain pacemakers or cochlear implants may be contraindicated, though device compatibility has improved significantly)

**Factors that reduce candidacy:**
- Active infection anywhere in the body
- Coagulopathy or inability to discontinue blood-thinning medications perioperatively
- Significant spinal anatomy abnormalities that would obstruct lead placement
- Prior spinal surgery with extensive scar tissue — not disqualifying but increases technical difficulty
- Ongoing unresolved legal proceedings related to the pain (can complicate outcome reporting)
- Uncontrolled psychiatric comorbidity

Patients with [chronic pain](/conditions/pain-syndromes/chronic-pain) driven primarily by central sensitization rather than a peripheral neuropathic generator may also see less benefit. A thorough pre-implant psychological evaluation is standard practice and is not a barrier — it is a tool that helps predict outcomes and ensures appropriate support is in place.

## The Trial Phase — A Critical Decision Point

Unlike most medical implants, spinal cord stimulators must be trialed before permanent implantation. This is not optional or merely procedural — the trial is the single most important predictor of long-term success.

During the trial, temporary leads are placed in the epidural space under fluoroscopic guidance using a needle technique, not surgery. The leads exit the skin and connect to an external trial generator that the patient wears for five to seven days during normal daily activities.

During the trial, the patient tracks their pain levels, activity, sleep quality, and medication use. The standard threshold for proceeding to permanent implant is at least 50% reduction in pain during the trial period, though many centers use 50% as a minimum and prefer to see 60–70% or better before recommending the permanent system.

If the trial is unsuccessful — meaning pain relief does not meet threshold or the patient finds the sensation intolerable — the temporary leads are simply removed in the office. No permanent changes have been made. The patient and physician then reassess whether to proceed, adjust the stimulation parameters and retry, or pursue a different treatment path.

A successful trial is not a guarantee that the permanent device will perform identically, but it is the best available predictor. Rushing through or skipping the trial is not standard practice at reputable pain practices.

## What the Permanent Implant Day Is Like

Permanent SCS implantation is typically performed as an outpatient procedure under monitored anesthesia care (light sedation), not general anesthesia. The leads are placed through a small incision or needle technique and anchored to prevent migration. The generator — about the size of a large matchbook — is implanted under the skin, most often in the upper buttock or lower abdomen, through a separate small incision.

The procedure generally takes sixty to ninety minutes. Most patients go home the same day or after a brief observation period. Activity restrictions apply for the first four to six weeks: no bending, twisting, or lifting over five to ten pounds while the leads anchor in place. Most patients can resume gentle walking immediately.

Post-procedure programming begins before discharge and continues in follow-up visits. The device is programmed wirelessly using an external programmer. Finding the optimal stimulation parameters takes time — expect to return for two to four programming sessions in the first several months.

## Living With an SCS — Programming, Adjustments, Limitations

Most patients with a successful SCS implant find that it becomes largely unremarkable in daily life within a few months. The device is rechargeable (rechargeable batteries last ten or more years) or non-rechargeable (replaced surgically every two to five years, depending on usage). Modern systems include patient controllers that allow adjustment of stimulation intensity within physician-set limits.

Practical considerations include:

- **MRI compatibility**: Many current SCS devices are conditionally MRI-compatible at 1.5T with the device turned off, but not all. Discuss imaging needs with your physician before implant selection.
- **Airport security and metal detectors**: The device may trigger security equipment. Patients receive a medical ID card. TSA screening protocols apply.
- **Driving**: Not permitted while stimulation is active in some systems. Policies vary by device and state law.
- **Contact sports and high-impact activities**: Generally discouraged due to lead migration risk.
- **Diathermy**: Deep-tissue physical therapy using diathermy is contraindicated with SCS.

The majority of patients who achieve a successful trial and proceed to permanent implant report meaningful, sustained reductions in pain and improved function. SCS does not eliminate pain in most cases — it reduces it to a manageable level that allows re-engagement with daily activities, physical therapy, and quality of life.
