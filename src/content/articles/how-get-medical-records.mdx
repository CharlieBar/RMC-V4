Medical records are more than paperwork — they are the official account of your health history, your injuries, your diagnoses, and your treatment. In the context of a workers' compensation claim, a personal injury case, or a transition to a new healthcare provider, your records become essential evidence and a critical communication tool. Understanding how to obtain them, what to ask for, and how they will be used can save you significant time and frustration when you need them most.

At Rand Medical Center in Arlington Heights, IL, we process records requests routinely for patients involved in [workers' compensation](/services/injury-services/workers-compensation), [personal injury](/services/injury-services/personal-injury), and [motor vehicle accident](/services/injury-services/motor-vehicle-accidents) cases, as well as patients transferring care or maintaining their own health files. This guide explains the full process — from your legal rights to the specific steps for requesting records — so you know exactly what to expect.

## Your Rights to Your Medical Records

Under the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA), you have a federally protected right to access and obtain copies of your medical records. This right belongs to you regardless of how your care was paid for, whether through health insurance, workers' compensation, a lien arrangement, or out of pocket.

Healthcare providers are required to provide access to your records within **30 calendar days** of a written request. Many providers, including Rand Medical Center, process requests significantly faster than this — typically within 5 to 10 business days for standard requests.

You have the right to receive records in the format you request, whether paper copies or electronic format. You may also request that records be sent directly to another provider, an attorney, an insurance carrier, or any other party you designate — as long as you have signed the appropriate authorization.

Healthcare providers may charge a **reasonable fee** for copying and processing records. Illinois law governs these fees, and they are generally modest for standard requests. Ask our office about current fees when you submit your request.

Your records cannot be withheld because of an outstanding balance. If you owe money on your account, that does not affect your right to access your own medical information.

## What Is Included in Your Medical Record

Understanding what your records contain helps you know exactly what to request and what will be most useful for your specific situation.

**Clinical notes (visit notes):** Written by your physician or provider after each appointment, these notes document your reported symptoms, the physical examination findings, your diagnosis, and the treatment plan. These are the primary record of every clinical encounter.

**Diagnostic imaging reports:** Written interpretations of any X-rays, MRI scans, CT scans, or other imaging performed or ordered through our office. Note that if imaging was performed at an outside facility, those records must be requested separately from that facility.

**Physical therapy records:** If you received [physical therapy](/services/physical-therapy) at Rand Medical Center, these records include your initial evaluation, session-by-session treatment notes, progress assessments, and discharge summaries.

**Procedure notes:** Detailed documentation of any interventional procedures such as injections, nerve blocks, or other [pain management](/services/pain-management) treatments, including the technique used and your immediate response.

**Laboratory results:** Results of any blood work, urinalysis, or other laboratory tests conducted through our office.

**Referral records:** Documentation of referrals to specialists or other providers, including the reason for referral and any reports received back from those providers.

**Narrative reports:** A narrative report is a separate document — not simply an extract from your chart — prepared specifically to describe your injuries, treatment, and prognosis in a comprehensive format suitable for legal and insurance proceedings. This is often the most important document in a personal injury or workers' comp case, and it is typically requested separately and prepared by your treating physician.

**Imaging media:** In addition to the written radiology report, the actual images (X-ray films or digital imaging media) can be provided on disc. For legal cases and specialist consultations, having the actual images is often important.

## How to Request Your Records

The process for requesting records from Rand Medical Center is straightforward. Here are the steps:

**Step 1: Contact our office.** Call us at [(224) 735-3522](tel:+12247353522) and ask to speak with the medical records department, or you can request records in person at our Arlington Heights location.

**Step 2: Complete a records release authorization form.** HIPAA requires a signed authorization before records can be released to anyone — including yourself in some circumstances. This form specifies what records are being released, to whom they are being sent, and the purpose of the disclosure. We will provide you with this form.

**Step 3: Specify exactly what you need.** Be as specific as possible. Indicate the date range you need, the types of records (visit notes, imaging reports, physical therapy records, etc.), the format you prefer (paper or electronic), and where the records should be sent. If you need records sent to an attorney, provide the attorney's name, firm, address, fax number, and email.

**Step 4: Allow processing time.** Standard requests are processed within 5 to 10 business days. If you have a deadline — a court date, a settlement conference, a deposition — inform us at the time of your request.

**Step 5: Confirm receipt.** If records are being sent directly to an attorney or insurance carrier, confirm with the recipient that they have received the complete file.

## Records for Workers' Compensation Cases

Workers' compensation cases involve specific documentation requirements, and the records requested in these cases are typically more comprehensive than a standard transfer of care request.

In a workers' compensation claim, your employer's insurance carrier (and potentially an independent medical examiner) will review your records in detail. The quality and completeness of your documentation directly affects how your claim is evaluated.

Records commonly needed for workers' comp cases include all visit notes from the date of injury forward, all diagnostic imaging and reports, physical therapy records including attendance and progress, procedure notes for any injections or other treatments, physician opinions on causation (whether your condition is work-related), work restriction documentation limiting your duties or requiring leave, and maximum medical improvement (MMI) determinations when applicable.

With your signed authorization, Rand Medical Center can send records directly to your workers' compensation insurance adjuster, your attorney, or a claims management service. We are experienced with the documentation standards required for workers' comp proceedings in Illinois.

If you were injured on the job and are receiving treatment at our facility, review our [workers' compensation services](/services/injury-services/workers-compensation) for more information on how we support injured workers through the claims process.

## Records for Personal Injury and Auto Accident Cases

For personal injury cases — whether arising from a car accident, a slip-and-fall, or another incident — your medical records are foundational to your claim. Insurance adjusters, opposing counsel, and ultimately juries use your records to evaluate the nature and extent of your injuries, the relationship between the accident and your symptoms, and the reasonableness of your medical treatment.

For [auto accident](/services/injury-services/motor-vehicle-accidents) and personal injury cases, attorneys typically request a complete set of records covering the entire treatment period. In addition to standard clinical records, a narrative medical report from your treating physician is often critical.

A **narrative medical report** prepared by your physician at Rand Medical Center will typically include:
- A description of your reported mechanism of injury
- Your initial presentation and clinical findings
- Your diagnosis and the basis for it
- A summary of all treatment provided
- Your response to treatment and current status
- An opinion on causation connecting your injuries to the accident
- Your prognosis and any permanent impairment or limitations

This document is authored specifically for legal purposes and is distinct from your routine clinical notes. It is prepared at your or your attorney's request and may involve an additional fee. If your attorney requests a narrative report, direct that request to our office as early as possible — well before any settlement deadline.

## Records for Transferring Care or Personal Files

Not every records request is for legal or insurance purposes. You may want your records to:

- Share with a new specialist or primary care physician
- Maintain your own complete health file
- Obtain a second opinion from another provider
- Move to a new geographic area and establish care elsewhere

For transfer-of-care requests, you will typically want your most recent visit notes, any relevant diagnostic reports and imaging, a current medication list, and any specialist reports. If you are seeing a new provider for a specific condition — [back pain](/conditions/spine/back-pain), [knee pain](/conditions/joints/knee-pain), or another ongoing issue — focus your request on records relevant to that condition.

We can send records electronically to receiving providers, which often speeds up the process of establishing new care.

## Practical Tips for Managing Your Medical Records

**Keep copies of everything.** Whether you are involved in a legal case or simply managing a health condition, maintaining your own file of medical records is good practice. You never know when you will need documentation of treatment that occurred years ago.

**Request records before treatment ends.** For ongoing cases, periodically request updated records rather than waiting until the very end of your treatment. This makes the final records compilation easier and gives your attorney time to review documentation before settlement discussions.

**Understand what you're requesting.** If you are unsure what records will be most useful for your attorney, ask them for a specific request list. Attorneys experienced in personal injury or workers' comp cases know exactly what documentation they need.

**Keep track of authorizations.** Each signed authorization is typically valid for a specific period and purpose. If you need records sent to multiple parties, you may need to sign separate authorizations.

**Check for accuracy.** When you receive your records, review them for accuracy. If you believe something is documented incorrectly, you have the right to request an amendment or add a statement of disagreement to your file.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long does it take to receive records?

Standard requests are typically processed within 5 to 10 business days. Expedited requests can often be accommodated with advance notice when a legal or insurance deadline requires faster turnaround. Call [(224) 735-3522](tel:+12247353522) to discuss your specific timeline.

### Can my attorney request my records directly?

Yes, with your signed authorization on file, your attorney can request records directly from our office. We work with personal injury and workers' compensation attorneys regularly and are familiar with the documentation standards these cases require. Make sure you have signed and provided an authorization to our office that specifically names your attorney's firm.

### Are there fees for obtaining medical records?

Illinois law allows healthcare providers to charge reasonable fees for copying and processing records. Fees may vary depending on the volume and format of the records requested. For large requests — such as complete case records for a legal proceeding — the fee may be higher than for a simple request for a single visit note. Contact our office at [(224) 735-3522](tel:+12247353522) for current fee information.

### What if I treated at multiple facilities?

If you received care at multiple locations — for example, an emergency room, a specialist's office, and a physical therapy clinic — you will need to submit a separate records request to each facility. Each provider maintains their own records independently. Your attorney can help coordinate multiple requests if you are involved in a legal case. At Rand Medical Center, our coordinated care model means that many of your records — from [pain management](/services/pain-management) consultations to [physical therapy](/services/physical-therapy) sessions to [diagnostic imaging](/services/diagnostic-surgical-services) — are available through a single request, which simplifies the process considerably.

### What is the difference between a records request and a narrative report?

A records request provides copies of existing clinical documentation — visit notes, test results, procedure notes. A narrative report is a new document authored by your treating physician specifically for legal or insurance purposes, summarizing your injuries, treatment, and prognosis in a cohesive format. Narrative reports are generally prepared at the request of the patient or their attorney and may involve a separate fee and longer preparation time.

## Understanding Your Rights Under Illinois Law

Under Illinois law, patients have a clear right to access their own medical records. The Illinois Medical Patient Rights Act and HIPAA both establish that you can obtain copies of virtually any documentation created during your care. Providers must respond to requests within a reasonable timeframe — typically 30 days under HIPAA, though most practices, including Rand Medical Center, process routine requests much faster than that.

It is important to understand that while providers can charge reasonable fees for copying and processing records, they cannot refuse to release your records simply because you have an outstanding balance. Your right to access your medical documentation exists independently of any billing relationship. If you encounter difficulty obtaining records from any provider, you can file a complaint with the Illinois Department of Public Health or the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services Office for Civil Rights.

For patients involved in [workers' compensation](/services/injury-services/workers-compensation) cases in Illinois, your employer's insurance company may also have a right to access records related to your workplace injury — but only records directly relevant to the claim, not your entire medical history. Understanding these boundaries helps protect your privacy while ensuring your claim proceeds smoothly.
