Most people associate an orthopedic doctor with joint replacement surgery, and that association does a disservice to one of medicine's most versatile specialties. In practice, the overwhelming majority of patients who see an orthopedic specialist never have an operation. They come in with a knee that won't straighten after a weekend run, a shoulder that clicks and aches through the night, or an ankle that has been "almost healed" for three months. They leave with a diagnosis, a structured plan, and — more often than not — a path back to full function that involves physical therapy, targeted injections, or simple activity modification.

This orthopedic doctor guide is designed to give you a complete picture: what these physicians actually treat, how to know when you need one, what a first visit looks like, and how the full arc of care unfolds from diagnosis through recovery. If you're in the northwest Chicago suburbs, it also explains how to find the right team close to home.

## What an Orthopedic Doctor Treats — Joints, Bones, Muscles, and Connective Tissue

Orthopedic medicine covers the entire musculoskeletal system — the interconnected framework of bones, joints, cartilage, tendons, ligaments, and muscles that lets you move through the world. That scope is wider than most patients realize.

On the bone side, orthopedic specialists manage acute fractures, stress fractures, bone infections, and metabolic conditions that weaken bone density over time. On the joint side, they evaluate and treat every major joint in the body: hip, knee, shoulder, elbow, wrist, ankle, and the small joints of the hands and feet. Cartilage damage, arthritis, labral tears, and joint instability all fall within this domain.

Soft tissue is equally central. Torn tendons — from the Achilles to the rotator cuff — ligament sprains, meniscus injuries, bursitis, and tendinopathy are bread-and-butter orthopedic diagnoses. So are overuse conditions like iliotibial band syndrome, patellar tendinitis, and plantar fasciitis.

What orthopedic doctors generally do not manage: nerve pain originating in the spine without a structural musculoskeletal component (that often belongs to neurology or pain management), systemic inflammatory conditions like rheumatoid arthritis in their early stages (rheumatology), and fractures or injuries that require emergency trauma surgery (trauma surgery). In practice, overlap exists, and a well-integrated clinic coordinates across these specialties seamlessly.

## When You Should See an Orthopedic Specialist (vs. PCP, vs. Pain Management)

The decision about which door to walk through first is genuinely confusing for patients. Here is a practical framework.

**See your primary care physician first when:** pain is new, mild, and unrelated to a specific injury event; when you need imaging ordered and interpreted before a specialist visit makes sense; or when the problem might have an internal medicine component (gout, for example, mimics joint pain but requires metabolic management).

**Go directly to an orthopedic doctor when:** you have a known or suspected fracture; a joint is visibly swollen, locked, or mechanically catching; you sustained a sports injury with immediate functional loss; or conservative self-care — rest, ice, over-the-counter anti-inflammatories — has not produced improvement after four to six weeks.

**Pain management may be the better route when:** pain is chronic, diffuse, or has a significant nerve component; when multiple body regions are involved; or when you've already had orthopedic evaluation and structural causes have been ruled out. At [Rand Medical Center](/services/pain-management), orthopedics and pain management work side by side, so patients with overlapping needs don't fall into the gap between specialties.

One rule of thumb worth internalizing: don't wait too long. A meniscus tear that might have been managed conservatively can progress to cartilage damage if left untreated for months. A shoulder injury that "should have gotten better by now" often has an underlying structural cause that imaging will reveal. Earlier evaluation almost always produces better outcomes.

## Common Conditions — Knee Pain, Shoulder Injuries, Arthritis, and Fractures

The conditions orthopedic doctors see most frequently cluster around a handful of high-load, high-use joints.

**Knee pain** is the single most common orthopedic complaint. Causes range widely: [osteoarthritis](/conditions/joints/knee-pain) in older adults, meniscus tears in active middle-aged patients, patellofemoral syndrome and ligament injuries in younger athletes, and bursitis or tendinopathy at virtually any age. Our guide to [strengthening exercises for knee pain](/blog/strengthening-exercises-knee-pain) covers the rehab side; this article focuses on the diagnostic and treatment pathway that makes those exercises effective.

**Shoulder injuries** are a close second. The rotator cuff — a group of four muscles and their tendons that stabilize and move the shoulder — is the site of injury most often. Partial and full-thickness tears, impingement syndrome, and biceps tendinopathy are all common. [Rotator cuff injuries and their treatment options](/blog/rotator-cuff-injuries-treatment) is worth reading alongside this guide. Labral tears and shoulder instability (recurrent dislocation) are also frequent, particularly in overhead athletes. [Shoulder pain](/conditions/joints/shoulder-pain) can present as deep aching, night pain, weakness, or a painful arc of motion — any of these warrants evaluation.

**Arthritis** in its many forms — most commonly osteoarthritis — affects nearly every large joint. [Arthritis](/conditions/joints/arthritis) is a degenerative process, but that does not mean it is untreatable or inevitably progressive. Early intervention with targeted physical therapy, weight management, and joint protection strategies can meaningfully slow functional decline. When conservative measures are exhausted, surgical options including joint replacement become relevant.

**Hip pain** deserves mention separately. [Hip pain](/conditions/joints/hip-pain) is frequently misattributed to the low back (and vice versa), which delays correct treatment. Hip labral tears, femoroacetabular impingement, osteoarthritis, and bursitis are the most common orthopedic hip diagnoses.

**Fractures** span the spectrum from minor finger avulsion fractures managed with splinting to complex femur fractures requiring surgery. Stress fractures — incomplete breaks from repetitive loading — are common in runners and military personnel and frequently missed on plain X-ray. [Diagnostic imaging through our services](/services/diagnostic-surgical-services) helps close that gap.

**Sports injuries** are addressed in their own section below, given how distinct the prevention-and-return-to-sport pathway is.

## Diagnostic Workup — Imaging, Physical Examination, and History

A rigorous orthopedic evaluation has three components that work in sequence, not in parallel.

**History comes first.** How the injury happened — or when the pain started — is often the most diagnostically useful piece of information an orthopedic doctor collects. Sudden onset with a distinct mechanism (a pop, a fall, a collision) points in a very different direction than insidious onset pain that has built slowly over months. Factors like age, activity level, prior injuries, and occupational demands all shape the differential diagnosis.

**Physical examination follows.** Orthopedic physical exam is highly specific — each test targets a particular structure. The McMurray test for the meniscus. The Hawkins-Kennedy test for shoulder impingement. The FABER test for hip pathology. A skilled orthopedic clinician can narrow the diagnosis significantly through examination alone, before a single image is taken.

**Imaging confirms and characterizes.** Plain X-rays are usually the first imaging study ordered — they're fast, inexpensive, and essential for evaluating bone. MRI is the gold standard for soft tissue: cartilage, tendons, ligaments, labra, and the spinal cord. Ultrasound is increasingly used for dynamic assessment of tendons and for guiding injections. CT adds detail for complex fractures or surgical planning. Our [diagnostic and surgical services team](/services/diagnostic-surgical-services) integrates imaging tightly with clinical evaluation so results inform the treatment plan the same day when possible.

## Conservative Treatment First — PT, Injections, and Bracing

The phrase "conservative treatment" sometimes gives patients the wrong impression — as if conservative means passive or ineffective. It doesn't. For the majority of orthopedic conditions, conservative care is the definitive treatment. Surgery is reserved for cases where it fails.

**Physical therapy** is the cornerstone of conservative orthopedic care. A targeted PT program strengthens the muscles surrounding an injured joint, restores range of motion, corrects movement patterns that predispose to re-injury, and reduces pain through mechanisms that don't involve medication. Our [physical therapy team](/services/physical-therapy) works directly alongside the orthopedic physicians at Rand Medical Center, which means the exercise prescription reflects exactly what the imaging and examination found — not a generic protocol.

**Injections** provide a different kind of conservative option. Corticosteroid injections reduce inflammation in bursae and joints when pain is limiting participation in physical therapy. Hyaluronic acid (viscosupplementation) injections can improve lubrication in arthritic joints. Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) is used in select cases for tendon and ligament injuries. Understanding which injection approach fits which condition — and how long to expect benefit — is covered in detail in our guide to [joint replacement vs. injections](/blog/joint-replacement-vs-injections).

**Bracing and orthotics** offload damaged structures, correct alignment, and protect healing tissue. Unloader braces for knee osteoarthritis, walking boots for stress fractures, and shoulder slings after rotator cuff repair all serve this function at different stages of care.

**Activity modification and pain education** round out the conservative toolkit. Knowing when to push through discomfort and when to rest is non-trivial, and getting it wrong in either direction slows recovery. Our [ice vs. heat guide](/blog/ice-vs-heat-injury-treatment) addresses one of the most common day-to-day management questions patients have during recovery.

## When Surgery Is the Right Choice (and When It Isn't)

Orthopedic surgery is indicated when conservative care has been genuinely exhausted and the patient's quality of life or functional capacity remains significantly impaired. "Genuinely exhausted" means an adequate trial — typically three to six months of structured PT, appropriate injections, and activity modification — not two weeks of half-hearted stretching.

There are exceptions where surgery should not wait. A complete ACL or rotator cuff tear in an active patient who wants to return to sport. An acute fracture requiring fixation. A locked knee with a displaced bucket-handle meniscus tear. Cauda equina syndrome from spinal compression. These are cases where conservative care would cause harm, not help.

For most other presentations — partial tendon tears, arthritic joints, mild-to-moderate meniscus tears — the evidence strongly supports conservative-first management. Our detailed comparison of [treatment options for meniscus tears](/blog/meniscus-tears-treatment-vs-surgery) and [joint replacement vs. injections](/blog/joint-replacement-vs-injections) work through the decision framework for two of the most common surgical crossroads patients face.

When surgery is the right call, preparation matters as much as technique. Pre-operative conditioning ("prehabilitation") shortens recovery time. Understanding what to expect after the procedure reduces anxiety and improves adherence to the rehab protocol. Our [diagnostic and surgical services](/services/diagnostic-surgical-services) are structured to support patients comprehensively through both phases.

## Sports Injuries Specifically — Prevention, Recovery, and Return to Sport

Sports injuries occupy a distinct place in the orthopedic landscape because the endpoint isn't just pain relief — it's safe return to athletic activity at the same or higher level of performance.

The recovery timeline depends heavily on the injury type, the athlete's baseline conditioning, and how aggressively (but responsibly) rehabilitation is pursued. Our [sports injury recovery timeline guide](/blog/sports-injury-recovery-timeline) breaks this down by injury category. What it won't tell you is whether you're ready to return — that requires objective functional testing, not just subjective pain ratings. "Feeling ready" is frequently not the same as "being ready," and returning too soon is the leading cause of re-injury.

Prevention deserves equal billing with treatment. The evidence for structured warm-up protocols — particularly neuromuscular activation work — in reducing injury rates is strong. Our [warm-up exercises guide](/blog/warm-up-exercises-prevent-injury) and [sports injury prevention guide](/blog/how-prevent-sports-injuries) give you actionable protocols backed by the same principles our sports medicine team applies clinically.

From an orthopedic standpoint, [sports injuries](/conditions/other/sports-injury) are best managed by clinicians who understand both the structural repair required and the performance demands of the sport or activity you're returning to. That context shapes every decision — how aggressive to be with rehab, when imaging is truly necessary, and when surgical intervention serves the athlete better than prolonged conservative management.

## Recovery and Rehab — The PT–Ortho Partnership

Recovery from orthopedic injury or surgery is not a passive process. The orthopedic physician's job is to create the structural conditions for healing — through diagnosis, injections, or surgery. The physical therapist's job is to rebuild the capacity that injury or surgery interrupted.

These two roles work best when they're tightly coordinated. When the PT and the orthopedic doctor are communicating regularly — reviewing the same imaging, sharing clinical notes, adjusting the plan as the patient progresses — outcomes improve meaningfully compared to siloed care.

Rehab doesn't end at your last physical therapy appointment; it continues as an ongoing investment in the capacity you worked hard to rebuild.
