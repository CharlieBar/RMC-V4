If you have been living with arthritic knee pain long enough, someone has probably raised the question: injections or surgery? For many patients, that question arrives at a crossroads — when over-the-counter medications are no longer enough, but the idea of joint replacement still feels like a last resort. Understanding how these two options differ, and when each one genuinely makes sense, helps you have a more productive conversation with your orthopedic physician.

This guide walks through both paths clearly, without oversimplifying the decision.

## When Injections Buy You Time — and Quality of Life

Joint injections are not a cure for arthritis. What they do offer is meaningful, sometimes substantial, relief from inflammation and pain — often enough to restore function and delay surgery by months or years.

The two most common injection types for arthritic joints are corticosteroid injections and hyaluronic acid (viscosupplementation) injections. Corticosteroids work quickly to suppress joint inflammation. Hyaluronic acid injections are designed to restore some of the lubricating cushion that degrades with arthritis. Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) injections represent a newer, evidence-evolving option that some patients pursue as well.

Injections tend to work best when:

- The arthritis is mild to moderate, not bone-on-bone
- Pain flares are episodic rather than constant
- The patient can participate in physical therapy alongside injections
- Age, health status, or activity level make surgery inadvisable in the near term
- The patient has specific events — a wedding, a grandchild's graduation, a travel commitment — that make a surgical recovery inconvenient right now

The honest caveat: injection relief is temporary. Most patients get three to six months of significant benefit from a corticosteroid injection, sometimes longer. Repeated injections over many years carry their own concerns, including potential cartilage effects, so they are best used strategically rather than indefinitely.

## When Joint Replacement Is the Right Call

Total or partial joint replacement is one of the most successful elective procedures in medicine. For the right patient at the right time, it reliably eliminates the pain source rather than temporarily managing it.

The clinical indicators that favor moving to joint replacement include:

- **Radiographic end-stage arthritis** — X-rays showing severe joint space narrowing or bone-on-bone contact
- **Constant pain** that no longer responds adequately to injections, medication, or therapy
- **Significant functional loss** — difficulty walking more than a block, inability to climb stairs, disrupted sleep from pain
- **Failed conservative care** — a documented course of physical therapy, anti-inflammatory medication, and at least one injection cycle without lasting improvement

Age is a factor, but not in the way patients sometimes assume. Younger patients (under 55–60) are sometimes counseled to delay replacement because implants have a lifespan of 15–20 years and revision surgery is more complex. Older patients in good health, however, are excellent candidates — recovery is very achievable and outcomes are well-documented.

## The Decision Matrix: Pain, Function, Imaging, Age, Activity Level

No single variable decides this question. Orthopedic physicians weigh several factors together:

| Factor | Favors Injections | Favors Replacement |
|---|---|---|
| X-ray findings | Mild–moderate joint space loss | Severe or bone-on-bone |
| Pain pattern | Episodic, manageable | Constant, disabling |
| Functional impact | Moderate limitations | Significant loss of ADLs |
| Response to prior injections | Good, lasting relief | Minimal or no relief |
| Age and implant lifespan | Younger, active patient | Older or less active |
| Medical fitness for surgery | Higher anesthetic risk | Cleared for surgery |

Your physician will also consider which joint is involved. Knee replacement outcomes are exceptionally well-studied. Hip replacement results are similarly strong. Shoulder replacement is more variable depending on the underlying diagnosis.

## What Realistic Outcomes Look Like for Each

**Injections:** Most patients report meaningful pain reduction within days of a corticosteroid injection. Hyaluronic acid takes longer — typically two to five weeks for full effect. Neither eliminates arthritis progression. You are managing symptoms, not the underlying joint condition.

**Joint replacement:** Recovery takes time — typically six weeks before driving, three months before most normal activity, and up to a year for full restoration of strength. However, the majority of patients report that joint replacement significantly improved or resolved their chronic pain. It is a longer upfront investment with a more durable payoff.

## Sequencing: Conservative First, Surgery When Indicated

The standard of care for arthritic joints follows a stepwise progression. Rushing to surgery before exhausting conservative options is rarely appropriate. Equally, continuing with injections that have stopped working — and accepting ongoing disability — does not serve the patient.

A reasonable sequence looks like this: anti-inflammatory medication and activity modification first, followed by supervised physical therapy to strengthen supporting muscles, then a course of injections if pain persists, and finally surgical evaluation when the above measures no longer maintain adequate function.
