## Understanding What Workers' Comp Covers

Before diving into the specifics of your medical visits, it helps to understand what the workers' compensation system actually covers. The Illinois Workers' Compensation Act requires employers to carry insurance that pays for all reasonable and necessary medical treatment related to a work injury. This is a no-fault system — meaning you don't need to prove your employer was negligent, only that your injury arose out of and in the course of your employment.

**Covered medical expenses typically include:**

- Physician and specialist visits
- Emergency and urgent care
- Diagnostic imaging (X-rays, MRI referrals)
- Prescription medications
- [Physical therapy](/services/physical-therapy) and occupational therapy
- [Pain management procedures](/services/pain-management) such as injections
- [Orthopedic care](/services/orthopedics) and surgical consultations
- Surgery when medically necessary
- Medical equipment such as braces, splints, or assistive devices
- Mileage reimbursement for travel to medical appointments

In addition to medical benefits, workers' compensation provides income replacement benefits if your injury prevents you from working — Temporary Total Disability (TTD) benefits equal to roughly two-thirds of your average weekly wage — as well as permanent disability benefits if your injury results in a lasting limitation.

## Do I Have to Pay Anything for a Workers' Comp Visit?

**No.** If your visit is related to an approved work injury, workers' compensation insurance covers the cost of all reasonable and necessary treatment. You should not receive a bill from your treating physician, specialist, physical therapist, or any other provider involved in your workers' comp care.

This is one of the areas where patients experience the most confusion. If you receive a bill, it may mean one of the following:

- The claim has not yet been formally approved by the insurance carrier
- There was a billing error in how the visit was coded
- The treatment provided was not pre-authorized when authorization was required
- Your treatment is being disputed as unrelated to the work injury

If you receive an unexpected bill, contact [Rand Medical Center's](/services/injury-services) billing team immediately. We have experience navigating workers' comp billing issues and can communicate directly with the insurance carrier on your behalf to resolve discrepancies.

## Can I Choose My Own Doctor for Workers' Comp in Illinois?

**Yes.** This is one of the most important rights under the Illinois Workers' Compensation Act. You have the legal right to choose your own treating physician for a work injury. Your employer may recommend a doctor, and in some cases may require you to attend an initial evaluation at a company-designated clinic, but you are not permanently bound to that provider.

Illinois law allows you **one physician change of your own choosing** during the course of your workers' compensation treatment. This means if you were sent to an employer-preferred doctor first, you can transfer your care to a physician you trust — such as the specialists at Rand Medical Center — without needing approval from your employer.

## What If My Employer Says I Need to See Their Doctor?

This situation causes a great deal of stress for injured workers. Here's what you need to know:

Your employer may **direct you to an initial evaluation** at a specific clinic or occupational health facility. You may choose to comply with this for your first visit, or you may decline and seek care with your own chosen physician from the start.

Your employer may also require you to attend an **Independent Medical Examination (IME)** — an evaluation conducted by a physician of the employer's or insurance carrier's choosing. You are generally required to attend these examinations when requested. However, the IME physician does **not** become your treating doctor. The IME physician evaluates you and provides a report to the insurance carrier — they do not manage your ongoing care.

Your **right to choose your own treating physician** is preserved regardless of whether you attended an employer-directed initial visit or an IME. If you feel your current workers' comp care is inadequate — if your doctor is not documenting thoroughly, is not issuing clear work restrictions, or is not treating your injury seriously — you have the right to transfer your care.

## What Do I Need to Bring to My Workers' Comp Visit?

Being prepared for your first workers' comp appointment ensures a smooth process and helps us create complete, accurate documentation from day one. Bring the following:

- **Your employer's name, address, and contact information** — we need this to send work status reports and communicate about your claim
- **Your workers' comp claim number**, if one has been assigned to your case by the insurance carrier
- **The date, time, and description of how your injury occurred** — write this down in advance so the account is consistent and detailed
- **The name and contact information of your employer's HR department or supervisor** who received your injury report
- **A photo ID**
- **Insurance information**, including your workers' comp carrier name and adjuster contact if you have it
- **Prior medical records** related to the injury, if you've already received treatment elsewhere
- **Any incident report** completed at the workplace after the injury

The more information you bring, the more complete and accurate your initial documentation will be. Thorough documentation from the first visit is one of the most important factors in a successful workers' compensation claim. For a detailed overview of the full claim process, see our [workers' comp process guide](/blog/workers-comp-process-guide).

## Will the Doctor Communicate With My Employer?

**Yes.** At Rand Medical Center, we provide regular, structured communication with your employer, case manager, and insurance adjuster throughout the duration of your workers' comp care. This includes:

- **Work status reports** issued after every visit, specifying your current diagnosis, treatment status, and any work restrictions
- **Responses to questions from the insurance adjuster** regarding your medical condition, treatment plan, and expected recovery timeline
- **Progress reports** documenting your functional improvements and changes in work capacity
- **Final medical reports** upon reaching Maximum Medical Improvement (MMI)
- **Coordination with your workers' compensation attorney**, if you have legal representation, including providing medical records and clarifying findings

One of the most common complaints among injured workers is that their doctor doesn't communicate clearly enough with their employer — leaving them in a difficult position when the employer questions their restrictions or pressures them to return to work prematurely. Our team takes this communication responsibility seriously. You should never be put in the position of verbally relaying medical information between your provider and your employer.

## What Happens If My Claim Is Disputed?

If the insurance carrier disputes your claim — either denying coverage entirely or disputing specific treatments — the matter may be referred to the Illinois Workers' Compensation Commission (IWCC) for arbitration. This is more common than many workers realize.

Having thorough, well-organized medical records from your treating physician is essential in these situations. Our documentation is specifically designed to be clear, objective, and legally defensible — describing the mechanism of injury, the clinical findings, the functional impact, and the medical necessity of each treatment provided.

If your claim is being disputed, consult with a workers' compensation attorney in addition to continuing your medical care. Your attorney can advise you on the legal process while our team provides the medical documentation that supports your case.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What if I have a pre-existing condition in the same area as my work injury?

A pre-existing condition does not disqualify you from workers' compensation benefits in Illinois. If your work injury aggravated, accelerated, or combined with a pre-existing condition to produce your current disability, you may still be entitled to benefits. This is called the "aggravation doctrine" under Illinois law. What matters is whether the work injury worsened your condition — not whether you had prior issues. Your treating physician at Rand Medical Center will document the relationship between your work injury and your current condition clearly and accurately.

### Can I see a specialist under workers' comp?

Yes. If your treating physician determines that specialist care is medically necessary, a referral for specialist evaluation is covered under workers' compensation. In many cases, your primary workers' comp treating physician will coordinate these referrals. At Rand Medical Center, we have [pain management specialists](/services/pain-management), [orthopedic consultants](/services/orthopedics), and [physical therapists](/services/physical-therapy) all within the same facility — meaning specialist care is coordinated efficiently without requiring multiple referrals to outside practices.

### What if I need surgery for my work injury?

Surgeries that are medically necessary for a work injury are covered under workers' compensation. Typically, the treating physician will first attempt conservative treatment and document that it has been inadequate before recommending surgical intervention. The insurance carrier may require pre-authorization for surgery, which your medical team will handle. At Rand Medical Center, we coordinate surgical consultations and can connect patients with appropriate surgical providers within our network. Our [diagnostic and surgical services](/services/diagnostic-surgical-services) team supports the full pre-operative and post-operative process.

### How do I know when I've reached Maximum Medical Improvement (MMI)?

Maximum Medical Improvement (MMI) is a determination made by your treating physician that your condition has stabilized — meaning further significant improvement is not expected with continued treatment. This doesn't necessarily mean you're pain-free; it means your condition has reached a plateau. MMI is an important milestone in the workers' compensation process because it triggers the disability rating process and may lead to settlement discussions. Your physician at Rand Medical Center will clearly explain what MMI means for your specific situation and what your options are once you reach that point.

## Know Your Rights as an Injured Worker in Illinois

The Illinois Workers' Compensation Act is designed to protect workers who are injured on the job. Key protections include the right to medical treatment at no out-of-pocket cost, the right to choose your own treating physician, the right to income replacement benefits if you cannot work, and protection from employer retaliation for filing a claim.

These rights exist on paper — but exercising them effectively requires having a knowledgeable medical team that documents your care thoroughly, communicates professionally with all parties, and understands the legal landscape of workers' compensation in Illinois. For a more detailed overview of your legal protections, see our article on [workers' comp rights in Illinois](/blog/workers-comp-rights-illinois).

If you're unsure whether you have grounds for a workers' compensation claim, or if your claim has been disputed, consulting with an Illinois workers' compensation attorney is always a wise step alongside continuing your medical care.
