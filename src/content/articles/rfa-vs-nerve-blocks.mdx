Patients considering interventional pain treatment often encounter two terms that sound similar but serve distinctly different purposes: nerve blocks and radiofrequency ablation (RFA). Both involve a needle, both target nerves, and both aim to reduce pain — but their mechanisms, durations, and appropriate uses are quite different. Understanding the distinction helps you ask better questions at your appointment and set realistic expectations for your results.

## Nerve Block: A Diagnostic and Short-Term Treatment Tool

A nerve block delivers a local anesthetic, sometimes combined with a corticosteroid, directly to a targeted nerve or group of nerves. The anesthetic temporarily interrupts the pain signal traveling along that nerve to your brain.

Nerve blocks serve two roles. First, they are diagnostic: if blocking a specific nerve dramatically reduces your pain for several hours, that tells your physician which nerve is the pain generator. This is valuable information that guides the next step in treatment. Second, nerve blocks can provide therapeutic relief — often lasting days to weeks, especially when a steroid is included to calm inflammation at the nerve root.

What nerve blocks do not do is create any permanent change to nerve tissue. The anesthetic wears off, the steroid effect fades, and the underlying pain source remains. For patients with acute flare-ups, nerve blocks can be enough to allow physical therapy and rehabilitation to take hold. For patients with chronic, recurring pain from structural sources, a nerve block is typically the beginning of a conversation, not the final answer.

Common targets for nerve blocks include facet joint nerves (medial branch blocks), epidural spaces along the spine, and peripheral nerves associated with specific pain syndromes. You can read more about the nerve block process in our dedicated article on [nerve blocks for chronic pain](/blog/nerve-blocks-chronic-pain).

## Radiofrequency Ablation: A Longer-Lasting Pain Solution

Radiofrequency ablation goes one step further. Rather than temporarily numbing a nerve, RFA uses precisely controlled radiofrequency energy to heat the nerve tissue to approximately 80°C, effectively disrupting its ability to transmit pain signals. The target nerve is not destroyed outright — it is lesioned in a way that interrupts signal conduction.

The clinical result is pain relief that typically lasts six to twenty-four months, sometimes longer. Because nerves can slowly regenerate, relief is not always permanent, but many patients undergo repeat RFA procedures successfully when pain returns.

RFA is most commonly applied to the medial branch nerves that supply the facet joints of the spine — the small joints connecting your vertebrae. Facet-mediated pain is extraordinarily common in both [back pain](/conditions/spine/back-pain) and neck pain, particularly in patients with degenerative disc disease or a history of whiplash. RFA is also used for sacroiliac joint pain, certain knee pain syndromes, and other peripheral pain generators.

## When Each Is the Right Call

The choice between a nerve block and RFA is not arbitrary — it follows a clinical logic:

**Choose a nerve block when:**
- The pain source has not been confirmed and diagnostic information is needed
- The pain is acute or subacute and may respond to short-term anesthetic and anti-inflammatory treatment
- Inflammatory pain is a major driver and a steroid component is appropriate
- The patient is preparing for surgery or physical therapy and needs a pain window to participate

**Choose RFA when:**
- Nerve blocks have already confirmed the target nerve as the pain generator
- The patient has had temporary but repeating relief from blocks, indicating the right nerve is being treated
- The pain is chronic and structural — meaning the underlying cause is not going to resolve
- Longer-duration relief is the goal to reduce the frequency of clinic visits

It is worth noting that RFA is almost never performed without a prior positive nerve block. The block serves as the qualifying test. This is not merely administrative — it is genuinely important clinical practice that increases the likelihood of a successful ablation and avoids lesioning a nerve that was not responsible for the patient's pain.

## What the Procedure Day Looks Like

Both procedures are performed under fluoroscopic (X-ray) guidance in a procedure suite. You will lie on a procedure table, the skin is cleaned and numbed with local anesthetic, and a thin needle is advanced to the target under real-time imaging. The physician confirms precise needle placement before delivering the treatment.

For a nerve block, once placement is confirmed, the anesthetic (with or without steroid) is injected. The procedure takes fifteen to thirty minutes and you typically go home the same day, though driving yourself is not advised.

For RFA, after confirming needle placement, the physician performs a stimulation test — applying a low-level current to confirm the needle is at the correct nerve and not adjacent to a motor nerve. Once confirmed, radiofrequency energy is applied for sixty to ninety seconds per nerve site. Multiple sites are often treated in the same session. Total procedure time is typically thirty to sixty minutes. Some patients experience a temporary increase in soreness at the treatment site over the first one to two weeks before relief develops — this is normal and expected.

## Combining Both — The Diagnostic-Then-Therapeutic Approach

In clinical practice, the most effective approach to facet-mediated or joint-mediated chronic pain often uses nerve blocks and RFA in sequence rather than as alternatives. The diagnostic nerve block identifies the pain generator and gives temporary relief. If the patient achieves meaningful reduction — typically defined as 50–80% improvement for the duration of the anesthetic — they are considered a strong candidate for RFA.

This stepwise approach is good medicine. It avoids performing an ablation on an uncertain target, manages expectations by letting the patient experience what RFA relief will feel like, and gives the care team objective data to document before proceeding to the more definitive treatment.

For patients managing [chronic pain](/conditions/pain-syndromes/chronic-pain) alongside physical therapy, this approach also creates therapeutic windows — periods of reduced pain during which PT exercises are more productive and rehabilitation advances faster.
