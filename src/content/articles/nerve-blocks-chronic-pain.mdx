Chronic pain has a way of taking over your life gradually — a burning leg, a neck that never stops aching, a sharp pain that flares with every movement and makes simple tasks feel impossible. When oral medications provide insufficient relief, or when side effects make them difficult to tolerate long-term, many patients wonder whether there is a more targeted approach available. **Nerve blocks** represent exactly that: precision pain relief delivered directly to the nerve or nerve group generating your symptoms.

For patients in Arlington Heights and the northwest suburbs, **Rand Medical Center** offers a comprehensive range of nerve block procedures performed by experienced pain management physicians. This guide explains what nerve blocks are, how they work, which types are used for different conditions, and what patients can realistically expect from the procedure and recovery.

## What Is a Nerve Block?

A [nerve block](/services/pain-management/nerve-blocks) is an injection of anesthetic medication — and often a corticosteroid — placed near a specific nerve or network of nerves. The medication temporarily interrupts the electrical pain signals traveling along that nerve to your brain. Without those signals reaching the brain, the pain they carry is reduced or eliminated.

Nerve blocks serve two distinct and equally important purposes:

**Diagnostic purpose:** If your physician suspects a particular nerve is generating your pain but needs to confirm it, a diagnostic nerve block provides the answer. By temporarily numbing that specific nerve and observing whether your pain disappears, the physician can confirm or rule out that nerve as the source — information that guides all subsequent treatment decisions.

**Therapeutic purpose:** When the pain-generating nerve is identified, a therapeutic nerve block delivers concentrated anti-inflammatory medication and anesthetic to reduce inflammation, interrupt the pain cycle, and provide lasting relief. For many patients, this targeted treatment provides relief that oral medications cannot match.

This dual role makes nerve blocks uniquely valuable among pain management interventions. They do not just treat pain — they help physicians understand it more precisely.

## How Nerve Blocks Work at the Biological Level

Pain signals travel through the nervous system via electrical impulses along nerve fibers. When tissue is injured or inflamed, nearby nerves carry those distress signals toward the brain, where they are interpreted as pain.

Local anesthetics used in nerve blocks work by **blocking sodium channels** on the nerve cell membrane. These channels are essential for generating and transmitting electrical impulses. When sodium channels are blocked, the nerve cannot fire — and the pain signal it would carry never reaches the brain.

Corticosteroids added to the injection address the underlying inflammation around the nerve itself. Inflamed nerves are hypersensitive — they fire more easily, with less provocation, and generate stronger pain signals than healthy nerves. By reducing this inflammation, corticosteroids can lower the nerve's sensitivity over time, providing relief that extends well beyond the duration of the local anesthetic.

For some chronic pain conditions — particularly [complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)](/conditions/nerves/complex-regional-pain-syndrome) and sympathetically maintained pain — nerve blocks targeting the sympathetic nervous system interrupt the abnormal pain amplification cycle that characterizes these disorders, providing relief that can last far longer than the pharmacologic duration of the medications injected.

## Types of Nerve Blocks and Their Applications

There is no single "nerve block" — the term encompasses a family of procedures targeting different nerves throughout the body. Your physician selects the appropriate type based on your diagnosis, symptoms, and imaging findings.

### Facet Joint Nerve Blocks (Medial Branch Blocks)

[Facet joint blocks](/services/pain-management/facet-joint-blocks) target the medial branch nerves that carry pain signals from the small facet joints along the spine. These joints are a common source of [back pain](/conditions/spine/back-pain) and [neck pain](/conditions/spine/neck-pain), particularly the dull, aching pain that is worse with extension and rotation and does not radiate significantly into the limbs.

A diagnostic facet block confirms whether these joints are generating your pain. If significant relief follows, a subsequent **radiofrequency ablation** procedure can provide longer-lasting relief by heating the medial branch nerves to disrupt their ability to transmit pain signals for 12 to 18 months or more.

### Selective Nerve Root Blocks

[Selective nerve root blocks](/services/pain-management/selective-nerve-root-blocks) isolate a single spinal nerve root at the point where it exits the spine. They are used when imaging shows multiple possible pain-generating levels and a physician needs to determine which specific nerve root is responsible for radiating arm or leg pain. They also provide therapeutic relief for conditions like [sciatica](/conditions/spine/sciatica), [radiculopathy](/conditions/spine/radiculopathy), and nerve root compression from [herniated discs](/conditions/spine/herniated-disc) or [spinal stenosis](/conditions/spine/spinal-stenosis).

### Intercostal Nerve Blocks

[Intercostal nerve blocks](/services/pain-management/intercostal-nerve-blocks) target the nerves running along each rib, which supply sensation to the chest and upper abdominal wall. They are used for chest wall pain following rib fractures, thoracic surgery, or herpes zoster (shingles). Post-herpetic neuralgia — the persistent burning pain that lingers after a shingles outbreak — responds particularly well to intercostal nerve blocks, especially when treatment is initiated early.

### Stellate Ganglion Blocks

The stellate ganglion is a cluster of sympathetic nerve tissue in the neck that controls nerve signaling to the face, neck, arm, and upper chest. A [stellate ganglion block](/services/pain-management/stellate-ganglion-block) is used for conditions involving the sympathetic nervous system in the upper body, including CRPS of the arm and hand, [neuropathy](/conditions/nerves/neuropathy) affecting the upper extremities, and certain vascular pain conditions.

The procedure is performed with ultrasound or fluoroscopic guidance to ensure precision and safety, given the anatomical complexity of the neck region.

### Trigger Point Injections

While technically targeting muscle tissue rather than a named nerve, [trigger point injections](/services/pain-management/trigger-point-injections) address the localized nerve hypersensitivity within tight, painful muscle knots (myofascial trigger points). These injections are used for [myofascial pain syndrome](/conditions/muscle/myofascial-pain), [fibromyalgia](/conditions/pain-syndromes/fibromyalgia) flares, and muscle-component back or neck pain.

## What to Expect During the Procedure

Knowing exactly what will happen during a nerve block reduces anxiety and helps patients participate more effectively in their own care.

**Positioning and preparation:** You will be positioned on a procedure table — lying face down, on your side, or sitting depending on the type of block. The skin over the injection site is cleaned with antiseptic solution.

**Imaging guidance:** Most nerve block procedures at Rand Medical Center are performed under **fluoroscopic (live X-ray) or ultrasound guidance**. This real-time visualization ensures the needle reaches precisely the intended target — a nerve or nerve cluster that may be only a few millimeters in diameter — safely and accurately. Imaging-guided nerve blocks have significantly better outcomes and safety profiles than unguided techniques.

**Local anesthesia:** Before the main injection, the skin and deeper tissues along the needle path are numbed with a small amount of local anesthetic. Most patients feel a brief sting and then pressure or mild discomfort as deeper tissues are reached.

**The injection:** The physician advances the needle to the target nerve under continuous imaging guidance. When the needle is confirmed to be in the correct position — sometimes verified with a small amount of contrast dye — the medication is injected. Patients typically feel pressure and sometimes a brief reproduction of familiar pain symptoms as medication reaches the nerve.

**Recovery:** Most nerve block procedures take **15 to 30 minutes**. You rest in a recovery area for 30 to 60 minutes while the team monitors your comfort and vital signs. Most patients go home the same day and can return to light activities within 24 hours.

You will need a driver for the day of the procedure, as some nerve blocks cause temporary numbness or weakness in nearby structures.

## How Long Does Relief Last?

The duration of relief from nerve blocks varies considerably based on the type of block, the underlying condition, and individual response:

- **Diagnostic nerve blocks** using local anesthetic alone typically provide **hours to a day** of relief — enough to confirm the pain source, not intended as ongoing therapy.
- **Therapeutic nerve blocks** combining local anesthetic and corticosteroid typically provide **weeks to months** of relief, with many patients experiencing meaningful improvement for two to six months.
- **Sympathetic nerve blocks** for CRPS or sympathetically maintained pain may provide **several weeks of relief per injection**, with repeated injections sometimes producing cumulative and progressively longer benefit.
- **Radiofrequency ablation** following confirmed diagnostic facet blocks provides **12 to 18 months or longer** of relief by denervating the pain-carrying nerve.

If your relief from a nerve block is short-lived, that does not mean the treatment failed — it means the targeted nerve was indeed your pain source, and your physician can now plan a more durable intervention accordingly.

## Conditions That Respond Well to Nerve Blocks

Nerve blocks are not appropriate for all types of pain, but for specific diagnoses, they are among the most effective interventions available:

- [Neuropathy](/conditions/nerves/neuropathy) — particularly peripheral neuropathy with a defined anatomical distribution
- [Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)](/conditions/nerves/complex-regional-pain-syndrome) — sympathetic nerve blocks are a cornerstone of CRPS treatment
- Facet-mediated [back pain](/conditions/spine/back-pain) and [neck pain](/conditions/spine/neck-pain)
- [Sciatica](/conditions/spine/sciatica) and spinal nerve root compression
- Post-herpetic neuralgia (shingles-related pain)
- Chest wall and rib pain from fractures or thoracic surgery
- Chronic pain that has not responded adequately to oral medications or physical therapy alone
- Pain from [herniated discs](/conditions/spine/herniated-disc) or [spinal stenosis](/conditions/spine/spinal-stenosis)

Nerve blocks are also valuable for patients who want to **reduce or eliminate their dependence on opioid pain medications** — targeted interventional therapy often allows significant medication reduction when it successfully controls the underlying pain source.

## Practical Tips Before and After Your Nerve Block

**Before the procedure:**
- Inform your doctor of all medications, especially blood thinners — many must be paused before the procedure
- Arrange a driver; do not plan to drive yourself home
- Eat and drink normally unless specifically instructed otherwise
- Wear loose, comfortable clothing appropriate to the injection site
- Notify your care team of any new symptoms, skin infections, or fever in the days before your appointment

**After the procedure:**
- Keep a pain diary for two to four weeks after the injection, noting pain levels and activities each day
- Avoid strenuous activity for 24 to 48 hours
- Do not apply heat to the injection site the day of the procedure
- Report fever, increasing redness or swelling, or any new neurological symptoms to your physician promptly
- Attend your follow-up appointment — your response to the block directly shapes the next phase of your treatment plan

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are nerve blocks the same as epidural steroid injections?

They are related but distinct procedures. **Epidural steroid injections** target the epidural space and affect multiple nerve roots broadly, making them better suited for generalized back or leg pain from disc herniation or stenosis. **Nerve blocks** target a specific named nerve or nerve group with greater precision, making them better for diagnosing or treating pain attributable to one identifiable nerve source. Many patients receive both types of injections as part of a comprehensive pain management plan. You can learn more about [epidural steroid injections](/blog/epidural-steroid-injections-guide) in our detailed guide.

### How many nerve block injections can I have?

There is no single universal limit. For **corticosteroid-containing nerve blocks**, most physicians recommend no more than three injections per site per year to avoid the effects of excessive corticosteroid exposure. For **local anesthetic-only diagnostic blocks** or **sympathetic blocks**, the frequency depends on your response and clinical picture. Radiofrequency ablation, when appropriate, reduces the need for repeated corticosteroid injections by providing much longer relief.

### Will the injection hurt?

Most patients describe nerve block procedures as **moderately uncomfortable but tolerable**. The local anesthetic applied to the skin and deeper tissues before the main injection significantly reduces discomfort. Some patients feel a brief, familiar pain sensation when the needle reaches the target nerve — this is actually diagnostically informative and expected. Post-procedure soreness at the injection site for one to two days is common and manageable with ice and over-the-counter analgesics.

### Can I combine nerve blocks with physical therapy?

Absolutely — in fact, this combination often produces the best outcomes. Nerve blocks reduce pain to a level that makes active participation in [physical therapy](/services/physical-therapy) more feasible and effective. When pain is well-controlled, patients can engage more fully with strengthening and mobility exercises that address the underlying functional deficits contributing to their condition. At Rand Medical Center, pain management and physical therapy are coordinated as part of an integrated care model.

## When to Seek Help for Nerve Pain

Nerve pain has distinctive characteristics that set it apart from muscle or joint pain: burning, electric, shooting, or stabbing sensations; numbness or tingling; sensitivity to light touch; and pain that travels along a defined path rather than staying localized. If you recognize these patterns in your own pain experience, a pain management evaluation is warranted.

Seek timely evaluation if:
- Pain has been present for more than four to six weeks without improvement
- Numbness or weakness is accompanying your pain — these neurological symptoms warrant prompt assessment
- Pain is worsening despite conservative treatment with rest and medications
- You have been told you have [neuropathy](/conditions/nerves/neuropathy), CRPS, or nerve compression and have not yet explored interventional options
- Pain is significantly limiting your work, sleep, or daily activities
