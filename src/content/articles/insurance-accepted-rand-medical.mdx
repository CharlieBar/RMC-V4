## Major Health Insurance Plans

Rand Medical Center accepts most major health insurance plans, including PPO (Preferred Provider Organization) and HMO (Health Maintenance Organization) plans from major carriers operating in Illinois.

**PPO plans** generally offer the most flexibility. Patients with PPO coverage can typically see specialists and obtain services without requiring a referral from a primary care physician, although some plans require pre-authorization for specific procedures such as imaging or interventional treatments.

**HMO plans** may require a referral from your primary care physician before you can see a specialist or receive certain services. If you have an HMO, check with your plan before your first appointment to determine whether a referral is needed.

**Medicare** is accepted at Rand Medical Center. Medicare patients should bring their red, white, and blue Medicare card to their appointment. If you have supplemental (Medigap) coverage, bring that card as well.

**Medicaid** — we accept many Medicaid plans. Coverage varies by specific plan and service type, so call our office in advance to confirm that your particular Medicaid plan is accepted for the services you need.

Because insurance networks, plan types, and coverage details change regularly, the most reliable way to confirm that your specific plan is accepted is to call our office at [(224) 735-3522](tel:+12247353522) before your first visit. Our billing team will verify your coverage and explain any copays, deductibles, or pre-authorization requirements that apply.

## Workers' Compensation Coverage

Rand Medical Center specializes in [workers' compensation](/services/injury-services/workers-compensation) cases and is well-versed in the documentation, billing, and reporting requirements of the Illinois workers' compensation system.

If you were injured on the job, your employer's workers' compensation insurance is responsible for covering your medical treatment. **You should not use your personal health insurance for a work-related injury** — doing so can complicate your claim and may affect the reimbursement your health insurer is entitled to.

Workers' compensation coverage for medical treatment works differently from standard health insurance:

**No out-of-pocket costs:** Approved workers' comp medical treatment is provided at no cost to you. There are no copays, no deductibles, and no bills for treatment that is authorized under your claim.

**Authorization requirements:** Depending on your employer's workers' compensation carrier, certain treatments — particularly procedures, specialty consultations, or extended physical therapy — may require pre-authorization from the insurance carrier. Our office handles this authorization process on your behalf and communicates directly with the workers' comp adjuster.

**Reporting requirements:** Illinois workers' compensation cases involve specific reporting requirements. Our physicians are experienced with generating the required forms, causation documentation, work restriction letters, and progress reports that workers' comp carriers need throughout the course of treatment.

**Returning to work:** Our physicians provide work status documentation throughout your recovery — including full duty release, modified duty restrictions, or temporary total disability certification when medically indicated. These documents are essential for managing your claim and communicating with your employer.

To initiate workers' comp treatment at Rand Medical Center, bring your claim number, the name of your employer's workers' compensation insurance carrier, and the contact information for your adjuster to your first appointment. Call ahead so we can verify authorization before you arrive.

Explore our [workers' compensation services](/services/injury-services/workers-compensation) and [workplace injury](/services/injury-services/workplace-injuries) information for more details on how we support injured workers.

## Auto Accident and Personal Injury Cases

Auto accidents and personal injury cases involve a different set of insurance mechanisms, and the options available depend on your specific situation.

### Auto Insurance Medical Payments (MedPay)

Many automobile insurance policies include a **medical payments provision**, commonly called MedPay. This coverage pays for medical expenses resulting from a car accident regardless of who was at fault. Coverage limits vary by policy but typically range from $1,000 to $25,000 or more.

MedPay is often an underutilized resource because policyholders are unaware it exists or do not realize it applies to their own medical treatment. Check your auto insurance policy or contact your insurance agent to determine whether you have MedPay coverage and what the limit is.

### Lien-Based Treatment

For qualified [auto accident](/services/injury-services/motor-vehicle-accidents) and [personal injury](/services/injury-services/personal-injury) cases, Rand Medical Center can often provide treatment on a **lien basis**. Under a lien arrangement, treatment is provided now and payment is deferred until your case reaches a settlement or verdict. A lien is a legal instrument that ensures medical providers are paid from the case proceeds.

Lien-based treatment is particularly valuable for patients who:
- Do not have health insurance
- Have health insurance that excludes auto accident injuries
- Are awaiting determination of fault and liability
- Have an attorney actively pursuing their case

Not every case qualifies for a lien arrangement. Our billing team evaluates cases individually. Contact our office to discuss whether lien-based treatment is available for your situation.

### Health Insurance for Accident Cases

Your standard health insurance can be used for personal injury treatment in many circumstances. However, there is an important consideration: if your health insurer pays for treatment that is later the subject of a personal injury settlement, they may assert a **subrogation claim** — a right to be reimbursed from your settlement proceeds. Your personal injury attorney should advise you on how to manage this.

Using health insurance while a case is pending is often the most practical approach, particularly for patients with good coverage and modest out-of-pocket costs. Your attorney and our billing team can help coordinate this appropriately.

## How to Verify Your Coverage

Insurance benefits can be confusing, and the only way to know exactly what your plan covers is to verify it directly. We recommend this verification process before your first appointment:

**Step 1: Call our billing team.** Contact Rand Medical Center at [(224) 735-3522](tel:+12247353522) and provide your insurance information. Our billing team will contact your insurer to verify that we are in-network for your plan, confirm your current deductible status and copay amounts, and identify whether any services you are likely to need require pre-authorization.

**Step 2: Understand your deductible.** Many health insurance plans have an annual deductible — an amount you must pay out of pocket before your insurance begins covering costs. Knowing where you are in your deductible year helps you anticipate your initial costs.

**Step 3: Understand your copay and coinsurance.** Even after meeting your deductible, most plans require a copay (a flat dollar amount) or coinsurance (a percentage of the billed amount) for each visit or service. Know these amounts before your first visit.

**Step 4: Ask about pre-authorization.** Certain services — MRI referrals, interventional procedures like injections, extended physical therapy — may require pre-authorization from your insurer. Our office obtains pre-authorization when required, but this process takes time. If pre-authorization is needed, schedule accordingly.

**Step 5: Bring your insurance card.** Always bring your current insurance card to your appointment. Insurance information changes, and we need the most current card to verify coverage and submit claims correctly.

## What to Bring to Your First Visit

Coming prepared with the right documents helps your first appointment run smoothly and prevents delays in initiating your care:

**For standard health insurance visits:**
- Valid government-issued photo ID
- Insurance card (front and back)
- Referral from your primary care physician if your HMO plan requires one
- List of all current medications, including dosages
- Any prior imaging on disc (X-rays, MRI) or access to a patient portal with your imaging records
- Medical records from previous providers relevant to your current condition

**For workers' compensation cases:**
- Photo ID
- Workers' comp claim number
- Name and contact information for your workers' comp insurance carrier
- Name and contact information for your adjuster
- Incident report or description of how the injury occurred
- Any prior medical records related to the work injury

**For auto accident and personal injury cases:**
- Photo ID
- Auto insurance card and policy information
- Police report number or copy (if applicable)
- Name and contact information for your personal injury attorney (if you have retained one)
- Health insurance card if you plan to use health insurance concurrently
- Documentation of any prior treatment received (ER records, urgent care records)

## Understanding Your Billing Statement

After your visit, you may receive a statement from Rand Medical Center showing the charges for your services, the amount billed to your insurance, and any balance remaining after insurance payment. Understanding this statement helps you identify discrepancies early.

**Explanation of Benefits (EOB):** Your insurance company will also send you an Explanation of Benefits document after processing your claim. This is not a bill — it is a summary of what was billed, what your insurer paid, and what you owe. Compare your EOB to your provider statement to confirm they match.

**Billing codes:** Medical services are billed using standardized procedure codes (CPT codes). If you have questions about what a specific charge represents, our billing team can explain every line item.

**Payment plans:** If you have a balance after insurance payment and need assistance, contact our billing team. We can discuss payment plan options. We are committed to making sure that financial considerations do not prevent you from receiving the care you need.

## Common Insurance Scenarios at Rand Medical Center

**"I was in a car accident but I have good health insurance. Should I use it?"**
You can use your health insurance for accident treatment, but also check whether you have MedPay coverage on your auto policy — it may cover some or all of your out-of-pocket costs. If you have a personal injury attorney, consult with them about the most strategic approach given your specific case.

**"My employer says I shouldn't file workers' comp — can I just use my health insurance?"**
You have a right to file a workers' compensation claim for a work-related injury, and employer discouragement of claims is illegal in Illinois. Workers' comp is specifically designed for work injuries and provides benefits — including wage replacement — that health insurance does not. Our [injury services](/services/injury-services) team can help you understand your rights and document your injury appropriately.

**"I don't have any insurance. Can I still be seen?"**
Depending on your situation, there may be options available — including lien-based treatment if you have a qualifying personal injury or auto accident case, payment plan arrangements, or community health resources. Call our office to discuss your circumstances. We want to help you access the care you need.
