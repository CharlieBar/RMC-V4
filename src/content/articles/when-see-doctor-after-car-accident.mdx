A fender-bender at a red light. A slow-speed rear-end tap in a parking lot. A glancing blow on the expressway. When the cars look fine and nobody is bleeding, it is easy to shake hands, exchange insurance cards, and drive home assuming everything is fine. Often that assumption is wrong.

The physics of even a low-speed collision can transmit significant force through the human body, especially the cervical spine, before any pain registers. Knowing **when to see a doctor after a car accident**—particularly a seemingly minor one—can be the difference between a smooth recovery and months of chronic pain.

## Why "Minor" Accidents Still Cause Real Injuries

Vehicle bumpers are engineered to absorb energy at low speeds. That engineering success actually works against the occupant: because the car sustains less visible damage, more of the collision force transfers directly to the people inside. Studies consistently show that occupant injury risk does not scale linearly with property damage. A 5 mph rear impact can generate enough neck acceleration to strain ligaments, compress facet joints, and irritate nerve roots—none of which will appear on the police report.

Soft tissue injuries—muscles, ligaments, tendons, and fascia—are especially deceptive. Inflammation builds over 24 to 72 hours. Many patients feel only mild soreness on the day of impact and wake up the next morning barely able to turn their head.

## 8 Symptoms That Mean You Should Be Evaluated

### 1. Delayed-Onset Neck Pain or Stiffness

If your neck feels fine at the scene but tight or sore by the next morning, that is a classic whiplash presentation. Do not dismiss it as "sleeping wrong." The cervical spine absorbed a rapid acceleration-deceleration event and the surrounding soft tissue is now inflamed. Early evaluation allows a physician to assess range of motion, palpate for muscle guarding, and determine whether imaging is warranted before the injury progresses.

### 2. Persistent Headaches

Post-collision headaches most commonly originate in the upper cervical joints and suboccipital muscles—not the skull. They can also reflect a mild traumatic brain injury (concussion), particularly if the head struck the headrest, airbag, or side window. Headaches that begin within 48 hours of impact and do not resolve with over-the-counter medication require a clinical evaluation.

### 3. Numbness or Tingling in the Arms or Hands

Radiating symptoms into the upper extremities suggest nerve involvement—either from a disc that shifted during impact or from compressed nerve roots in the neck. This is not a symptom to monitor at home. Nerve irritation that goes untreated can evolve into persistent neuropathic pain.

### 4. Abdominal Tenderness or Distension

Low-speed impacts can cause seatbelt contusion injuries to internal organs. The spleen and bowel are particularly vulnerable. Abdominal symptoms that develop in the hours after a collision—tenderness, bloating, pain that worsens on movement—warrant same-day evaluation because internal bleeding can be life-threatening and symptoms may be subtle at first.

### 5. Dizziness or Balance Disturbance

Vertigo and lightheadedness after a crash can stem from vestibular disruption, cervical muscle dysfunction, or concussion. None of these self-resolve reliably without treatment. If you feel unsteady walking to your car or find the room spinning after the accident, go directly to an urgent care or emergency facility.

### 6. Sleep Disturbance

Difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or feeling unrested after a collision is underreported but clinically meaningful. Pain disrupts sleep architecture. A physician can identify whether the sleep issue is purely pain-driven—and therefore addressable through injury treatment—or whether a neurological component needs attention.

### 7. Behavioral or Cognitive Changes After Head Impact

Irritability, difficulty concentrating, sensitivity to light or sound, and memory lapses that appear after the head makes contact with any surface are concussion symptoms until proven otherwise. The post-concussive period is a critical window. Activity restrictions and cognitive rest during this time reduce the risk of prolonged symptoms.

### 8. Ongoing Back Stiffness

The lumbar spine is the second most common injury site after rear-end collisions, behind the cervical spine. Stiffness, aching, or sharp pain with bending or sitting that persists beyond 24 hours suggests lumbar soft tissue strain or, less commonly, a disc injury. Early physical therapy intervention is significantly more effective than waiting until pain becomes severe.

## The 72-Hour Evaluation Window

The phrase "evaluation window" describes a clinical reality: many soft tissue injuries respond best to treatment initiated within the first three days of impact, before significant scar tissue formation or compensatory movement patterns develop. Equally important from a legal standpoint, a documented medical visit within 72 hours establishes the causal link between the collision and your injuries. Gaps in treatment give insurance adjusters grounds to argue the injury was pre-existing or unrelated.

## What the Visit Looks Like

A post-accident evaluation at Rand Medical Center typically involves:

- **History**: mechanism of injury, symptoms, prior conditions
- **Physical examination**: cervical and lumbar range of motion, neurological screening, palpation for muscle guarding
- **Imaging decision**: X-ray is often ordered at the first visit to rule out fracture; MRI may be ordered if neurological symptoms are present or if soft tissue injury is suspected (see our companion article on [MRI vs. X-ray for auto accident injuries](/blog/mri-vs-xray-auto-accident))
- **Treatment plan**: same-day referral to physical therapy if appropriate, medication if needed, follow-up scheduled

The visit is thorough but not lengthy. Most patients are in and out within an hour.

## Do Not Wait for Pain to Become Severe

Pain is a lagging indicator of injury severity. The absence of significant pain on Day 1 does not mean the injury is minor—it means your body has not yet mounted its full inflammatory response. By the time pain peaks, the opportunity for the earliest and most effective treatment window has often passed.
