Starting anything new in a medical setting can feel intimidating — especially when you are already dealing with pain, limited mobility, or the lingering effects of an injury. If you have been referred to [physical therapy](/services/physical-therapy) and are not sure what to expect, you are not alone. Many patients arrive for their first session uncertain, a little anxious, and full of questions.

The good news: your first physical therapy visit is designed specifically to address all of that. It is a structured conversation and assessment — not a workout, not a series of painful exercises, and certainly not an experience where someone pushes you through movements that hurt. By the end of the hour, you will have a clear picture of what is driving your pain or dysfunction, what your treatment plan looks like, and exactly what steps come next.

At **Rand Medical Center** in Arlington Heights, IL, our physical therapists are committed to making your first visit informative, comfortable, and productive. Here is a detailed walkthrough of everything that happens — from the moment you check in to the moment you leave with your personalized plan in hand.

## Before You Arrive: What to Prepare

The more information you bring to your first appointment, the more productive it will be. Your therapist needs to understand the full context of your condition — not just the symptom you are treating today, but the history behind it.

**Medical records and imaging.** If you have had X-rays, MRIs, or CT scans related to your condition, bring copies or ensure they have been forwarded to the clinic ahead of your visit. Your therapist will use these to understand the structural basis of your symptoms and to avoid movements that could be contraindicated.

**Your surgeon's or physician's referral.** If you were referred by a physician, bring that referral and any specific instructions or precautions your doctor has noted. For post-surgical patients, this might include weight-bearing restrictions, movement limitations, or a specific protocol your surgeon wants followed.

**Medication list.** Write down all medications you are currently taking, including over-the-counter pain relievers, anti-inflammatories, and supplements. Some medications affect pain perception, healing, and cardiovascular response to exercise, all of which are relevant to how your therapist designs your program.

**Insurance card and identification.** Arrive a few minutes early to complete any administrative paperwork. Many clinics offer digital intake forms you can complete at home — ask when you schedule.

**Comfortable clothing.** Wear or bring loose, flexible clothing that allows access to the area being treated. For lower extremity conditions like [knee pain](/conditions/joints/knee-pain) or [hip pain](/conditions/joints/hip-pain), shorts are ideal. For shoulder or upper back issues, a tank top or loose short-sleeved shirt works best. For [back pain](/conditions/spine/back-pain), wear pants or shorts you can easily move around in. Leave jewelry at home.

## Your First Visit Is an Evaluation, Not a Workout

If you have never been to [physical therapy](/services/physical-therapy) before, you might picture someone pushing you through painful exercises from minute one. The reality is much different — and much more comfortable.

Your first visit is almost entirely devoted to **evaluation**. This is the foundation of physical therapy — the process of identifying exactly what is wrong, why it is wrong, and what needs to change to fix it. Without a thorough evaluation, therapy is just guesswork. With it, every exercise and technique in your program has a specific, evidence-based purpose.

The evaluation takes longer than your follow-up sessions — typically 45 to 60 minutes compared to 30 to 45 minutes for ongoing visits. Your therapist may begin some gentle hands-on treatment during this first session if appropriate, but the emphasis is on learning, not loading.

## What Happens During the Evaluation

Your evaluation unfolds in a predictable, organized sequence. Understanding each component helps you participate more effectively and communicate your situation clearly.

### Medical History and Symptom Interview

Your therapist begins by reviewing your medical history and asking detailed questions about your current condition. This interview is more thorough than it might seem — every detail matters.

Expect questions about:
- When the pain or problem began, and whether it started suddenly or developed gradually
- What makes it better and what makes it worse
- How it affects your daily activities — work tasks, sleep, driving, household chores
- Previous episodes of the same issue and how they resolved
- Previous treatments you have tried, including injections, medications, or prior physical therapy
- Your activity level and fitness background
- Your goals: what do you want to be able to do that you cannot do right now?

That last question — your goals — is particularly important. A 65-year-old with [arthritis](/conditions/joints/arthritis) who wants to walk her grandchildren to school every morning has different targets than a 35-year-old competitive runner returning from a hamstring tear. Your therapist uses your goals to set meaningful milestones and measure progress in terms that matter to you personally.

### Physical Assessment

After the interview, your therapist performs a hands-on physical examination. This typically includes:

**Posture and movement analysis.** Your therapist observes how you stand, sit, walk, and perform specific movements. Compensatory patterns — the way your body unconsciously shifts to protect a painful area — often reveal as much about the root cause as the pain location itself.

**Range of motion testing.** Using a goniometer (a tool that measures joint angles) or visual assessment, your therapist measures the mobility of affected joints and compares it to normal values and to the opposite side of your body. Restrictions in range of motion often correlate directly with functional limitations.

**Strength testing.** Manual muscle testing identifies weakness in specific muscle groups that may be contributing to your problem. For example, weak hip abductors are a common driver of [knee pain](/conditions/joints/knee-pain), even when the knee itself appears structurally sound. Identifying these deficits allows the therapist to target the true source, not just the site of symptoms.

**Neurological screening.** If there is any concern about nerve involvement — such as radiating pain, numbness, or tingling — your therapist will assess sensation, reflexes, and nerve tension signs. This helps differentiate musculoskeletal pain from conditions like [sciatica](/conditions/spine/sciatica) or [spinal stenosis](/conditions/spine/back-pain) that require a different treatment approach.

**Special orthopedic tests.** Depending on your condition, your therapist may perform standardized tests specific to your area of complaint — Ober's test for IT band tightness, the McMurray test for meniscal injury, the Spurling's test for cervical nerve compression, and many others. These tests help confirm or rule out specific diagnoses.

**Functional tasks.** You may be asked to perform task-based movements such as rising from a chair, climbing a step, reaching overhead, or demonstrating a work-related posture. These reveal how your impairments translate to real-life limitations.

## Your Personalized Treatment Plan

Based on everything gathered in the evaluation, your therapist designs an **individualized treatment plan** — not a generic protocol pulled from a template, but a specific program built around your diagnosis, your deficits, your goals, and your lifestyle.

This plan includes:

**Specific treatment goals** written in measurable, functional terms. Instead of "reduce pain," you might see "increase knee flexion to 120 degrees to allow comfortable stair climbing" or "improve lumbar mobility to return to gardening without pain."

**Treatment interventions** — the specific techniques and exercises your therapist will use. These may include therapeutic exercise, manual therapy (hands-on techniques to mobilize joints and soft tissue), modalities such as ultrasound or electrical stimulation, neuromuscular re-education, and functional training.

**Session frequency and estimated duration** — how often you will come in and approximately how long your course of treatment will last. For most outpatient conditions, two to three sessions per week for four to eight weeks is a common starting framework, though this varies widely by diagnosis.

**A home exercise program (HEP).** This is one of the most important components of physical therapy and one that many patients underestimate. Your HEP consists of exercises you perform between sessions to maintain and accelerate the gains made in clinic. Your therapist will teach you each exercise on the first visit, demonstrate it, watch you perform it, and correct your form before you leave. Consistency with your home program is one of the strongest predictors of recovery speed.

## What Treatments Might Begin on Day One

Depending on your condition and what the evaluation reveals, your therapist may begin some gentle treatment during your first visit. This might include:

**Manual therapy.** Soft tissue mobilization, joint mobilization, or myofascial release techniques to begin addressing tissue restrictions, muscle guarding, and joint stiffness. These hands-on techniques often produce immediate improvements in comfort and mobility.

**Neuromuscular electrical stimulation (NMES) or TENS.** Electrical stimulation can reduce pain, decrease muscle spasm, and begin activating muscles that have become inhibited due to injury or surgery.

**Therapeutic exercise.** A small set of introductory exercises — chosen for low risk and immediate relevance — may be demonstrated and practiced during the first session. These will also form the basis of your initial home program.

**Postural and movement education.** Your therapist may identify specific habits — how you sit at your desk, how you lift objects, how you sleep — that are perpetuating your problem, and begin educating you about modifications that reduce daily strain.

By the end of your first visit to **Rand Medical Center**, you will not leave with vague reassurances or a stack of generic handouts. You will leave with:

## Practical Advice for Getting the Most From Physical Therapy

Physical therapy is a **participatory treatment**. The quality of your outcomes is directly related to the quality of your engagement. Here is what makes the difference:

**Do your home exercises every day.** The exercises your therapist assigns are not optional extras — they are core components of your treatment. Patients who complete their home programs consistently recover significantly faster than those who do them occasionally.

**Be honest about pain.** Report pain levels accurately during each session. Physical therapy should never produce sharp, new, or escalating pain. If it does, your therapist needs to know immediately so the program can be adjusted.

**Ask questions.** If you do not understand why you are doing an exercise, ask. Understanding the purpose of each movement improves adherence and helps you perform it correctly.

**Communicate lifestyle changes.** If your symptoms changed after a long car trip, a bad night's sleep, or a stressful week at work, tell your therapist. Context shapes the session and helps the therapist interpret your progress accurately.

**Trust the process, but speak up if something feels wrong.** Physical therapy is evidence-based and progressive. There will be sessions where you feel challenged and sessions where you feel like you are not doing much. Both have clinical purpose. But if you consistently feel that therapy is not helping or is making things worse, raise it — adjustments can and should be made.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need a doctor's referral for physical therapy?

In Illinois, you can access physical therapy without a physician's referral for an initial evaluation and up to 10 visits under direct access laws. However, many insurance plans require a referral for coverage, and conditions involving recent surgery, fracture, or neurological symptoms benefit from physician coordination. Check with your insurance and ask the **Rand Medical Center** scheduling team when you call.

### Will physical therapy hurt?

Physical therapy should not cause sharp or severe pain. You may experience mild muscle soreness after sessions — similar to how you feel after a workout you have not done in a while — and some techniques involve temporary discomfort. Your therapist will always explain what you should feel and ask for feedback. Pain beyond mild soreness is a signal to stop and communicate.

### How many visits will I need?

This depends entirely on your diagnosis and goals. Mild conditions such as [sprains and strains](/conditions/muscles/sprains-strains) may resolve in 6 to 8 visits. Moderate musculoskeletal conditions often require 12 to 16 visits. Post-surgical rehabilitation can extend to 20 to 30 visits over several months. Your therapist will provide an estimate at your first visit and reassess it regularly as you progress.

### What if I have been to physical therapy before and it did not help?

Previous PT experience — positive or negative — is important information for your new therapist. If prior therapy did not help, your therapist will want to understand what was tried, how long it lasted, what your response was, and what has changed since. Often, a more precisely targeted program or a different combination of interventions produces results where previous attempts fell short.

## When to Seek Additional Help Beyond Physical Therapy

Physical therapy is highly effective for the vast majority of musculoskeletal and neuromuscular conditions. However, some presentations indicate that additional medical evaluation is needed alongside or instead of PT.

Seek prompt evaluation if you experience:
- **Sudden severe pain** following an activity or incident that was not present before
- **Numbness, weakness, or coordination changes** in your limbs that are new or worsening
- **Loss of bladder or bowel control** — a potential sign of serious spinal nerve compression
- **Unexplained weight loss, fever, or night sweats** accompanying musculoskeletal symptoms
- **Symptoms that are not improving** after 4 to 6 weeks of consistent physical therapy
